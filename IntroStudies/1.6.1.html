The Bible Speaks to Us

Deeper Understanding of Scripture

10) The New Testament Writers and Scripture

When Jesus and the Apostles referred to "Scripture," they meant what we now call the Old Testament.

John 5:39, 2 Timothy 3:16, 2 Peter 3:16

At that time, the New Testament had not yet been written. When they began to write their letters and books, they were not even aware that they were beginning to compose what we now call the New Testament. So, when they used the term "Scripture," they were thinking of the Old Testament. They considered it their Bible—the Bible of Jesus, the Apostles, and the first Christians. They loved its stories, its message, and its prophecies.

As Christians, following their example, we should not think that the Old Testament has no value for us. These writings are also Christian writings because they come from the same God who inspired the New Testament, and at their center is the same Jesus. Therefore, the skepticism some Christians have toward the Old Testament, as if it were something that does not concern them, is not justifiable.

It is true that, coming from an older time and a world very different from ours, we sometimes find it more difficult to understand. However, this is a challenge that can be overcome through study.

The first writing of what would later become the New Testament seems to be Paul's first letter to the Thessalonians, written in 51 AD. The first person to place a New Testament writing on the same level as the ancient Scriptures was Peter, referring to some of Paul's writings (2 Peter 3:15-16).

The letters and books of the New Testament were generally written for a specific group of believers, for one or more churches in a particular region. Other churches had to wait some time before they too had access to a copy. The process of collecting all these writings into a single, universally recognized collection took at least a couple of centuries.

11) The Bible and Tradition

Key Points on Tradition:

The Catholic Church places great importance on Tradition. We can certainly say that many Catholic beliefs are based on it. But what is it? Where does it come from?

The Role of Oral Tradition

The Catholic Church teaches that Jesus did not write anything and that His message was initially transmitted orally. Catholics say that when Christians began to write, they did not record everything (John 20:30-31; 21:25). In this way, part of Jesus' teaching would have remained in oral form and would have been transmitted to us through what is called "oral Tradition."

This Tradition, according to Catholics, has been preserved by the Church up to our times. Likewise, the teachings of the apostles would have also been preserved. Additionally, everything that the Church has acquired over the centuries would have been received under the guidance of the Holy Spirit.

Evaluating the Teaching
    1. It is true that Jesus did not write anything and that He entrusted His disciples with the task of bearing witness.
    2. It is also true that His disciples did not write everything that Jesus did.
    3. However, it is wrong to think that not all of Jesus' teachings have been transmitted to us. John writes that not all miracles were recorded, but he also states that what he did write is sufficient for believing and obtaining eternal life. If his writings alone were already sufficient, what should we say when we consider that we have three other Gospels and many additional writings on Jesus' teachings? Certainly, we have everything we need, even without any oral Tradition.
    4. It is wrong to base our faith on oral traditions because they can easily be altered.
    5. To ensure that His teaching was faithfully transmitted, Jesus did not entrust its preservation to an indistinct crowd but chose 12 eyewitnesses called Apostles (Mark 3:14; Acts 1:20-21). Their testimony is preserved in the books that make up the New Testament. Shortly after the death of the Apostles, many stories and teachings began to spread within the Church that had nothing to do with the truth of Jesus and the Apostles themselves. To prevent these ideas from contaminating the pure Christian faith, the Church carefully selected the writings that were circulating, choosing only those that undoubtedly came from the Apostles and their direct disciples. In this way, they adhered to a written rule: the Canon (= norm) of the New Testament. Following their example, we reject any teaching that contradicts the canonical writings.
    Jewish Traditions and Jesus' Teachings

    The Jews also believed that God had given Moses many laws not included in the written Law, and they thought these additional rules had been orally transmitted from generation to generation until the time of Jesus.

    In reality, these rules did not come from Moses at all but were the result of a later development of their beliefs. Many times, these teachings contradicted or altered what was found in the Scriptures. Jesus firmly rejected them and taught that the Scriptures should be followed to uphold and preach God's will (Matthew 15:1-9).

    The Guidance of the Holy Spirit

    Regarding the guidance of the Holy Spirit, we must note that Jesus did not say that He would teach different things but that He would remind and help us understand what He had already said (John 14:17). Even in what may be considered new, the Spirit never contradicts what He has already given through the previous prophets and apostles (1 Corinthians 14:32; Malachi 3:6).

12) Authority to Interpret Scripture

Scripture Interpretation: Contrasting Views

Catholic Teaching:

Only the Pope and bishops can officially interpret Scripture.

Biblical Perspective:

The Bible is addressed to all believers, who are personally responsible for studying it.

1 Thessalonians 5:21; Acts 17:10-11

The Church is meant to be a supportive community, where believers:

  • Study God's Word together
  • Share insights and understanding
  • Approach Scripture with humility
  • Accept personal responsibility for learning
