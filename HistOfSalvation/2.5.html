Isaiah 53: The Gospel in the Old Testament

Purpose:

To understand one of the greatest and most beautiful prophecies of the Old Testament, to increase our trust in God and better comprehend the eternal meaning of His love and the significance of Jesus' death.

Introduction

We already know many important things about Jesus as our Savior, but we can discover much more. As we have studied, God promised a Savior from the first moment it became necessary: immediately after humanity's first sin (Genesis 3:14,15). Subsequently, this promise was better specified and reinforced.

One of the highest points of Messianic expectation comes through the prophet Isaiah. His description of God's salvation offered through Jesus is so profound and clear that an attentive reader might wonder if, instead of being a prophet, he was directly one of the Lord's disciples, an eyewitness to the events. It is indeed as if Isaiah had seen with his own eyes what happened to Jesus, and we know this was true, though the eyes were not physical but spiritual ones illuminated by the Holy Spirit. Isaiah received a special prophetic revelation from God who knows all things before they happen (Isaiah 42:9; 46:10).

In this study, we will consider chapter 53 of this prophet's book. We should approach this text with respect, in an attitude of meditation, to learn new things about God's love and place our hope in Him. We will follow the text of the New Revised Version, but any version is suitable. It's just useful to consider that, being a poetic text, some translations might render certain expressions differently.

1) How is the Savior introduced?

Isaiah 53:1-3

"Who has believed our message? And to whom has the arm of the LORD been revealed? He grew up before him like a tender shoot, like a root out of dry ground. He had no beauty or majesty to attract us to him, nothing in his appearance that we should desire him. He was despised and rejected by mankind, a man of suffering, and familiar with pain. Like one from whom people hide their faces he was despised, and we held him in low esteem."

Notes:

Verse 1:

Through two rhetorical questions, the prophet clarifies that what he has already said (see verses 13-15 of the previous chapter, where this Messianic passage begins) and what he is about to say about how God chose to save His people, is totally different from what humanity could have imagined or hoped for. God's plan is so surprising as to become incredible. We must therefore renounce all our rationality and conceptions to embrace God's offer wholeheartedly.

Verse 2a:

The Savior's life is not easy from His first appearance. Nothing in this world favors His rise and success. The world is like a hostile desert to Him, yet He accepts coming among us. We can think of Herod's attempt to suppress Him at birth (Matthew 1:16-18) and also the overall hostility of His own people and the world (John 1:5,10-11).

Verse 2b:

The Savior does not seek to conquer the world with His appearance, with human glory too often tainted by superficiality and violence. The Gospel is substance that appeals to the heart and love's power capable of transforming it.

Verse 3:

The world is not capable of accepting such a Savior, rejects Him, and this makes Him suffer. The text may also refer to Jesus' appearance during crucifixion, when all glory, human and divine, was totally absent and people looked at Him with dismay (His disciples) or with contempt (Luke 23:35-37). How would we have reacted before Jesus' cross? How do we react now?

2) In what incredible way will the Savior offer us His salvation?

Isaiah 53:4-6

"Surely he took up our pain and bore our suffering, yet we considered him punished by God, stricken by him, and afflicted. But he was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities; the punishment that brought us peace was on him, and by his wounds we are healed. We all, like sheep, have gone astray, each of us has turned to our own way; and the LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all."

Notes:

  • a) Consider how Jesus was tortured and killed. Can we see the cross in these verses?
  • b) In the past, and often even now, it was thought that a wealthy and happy person, someone healthy and successful, was so because they were righteous and thus blessed by God. Conversely, someone who suffered was considered cursed because they were a sinner (John 9:2; Job 8). Thus, the Messiah, because of His sufferings, was considered rejected and punished by God. Instead, says the prophet, if He suffers, it is because of our sins, not His own.
  • c) The gospel is this: we deserve death because of our sin, but the Messiah offers His life in place of ours. 1 Peter 3:18: "For Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God."

3) What attitude did the Savior have in His suffering?

Isaiah 53:7

"He was oppressed and afflicted, yet he did not open his mouth; he was led like a lamb to the slaughter, and as a sheep before its shearers is silent, so he did not open his mouth."

Jesus was not forced by anyone. His was a sacrifice freely and lovingly lived. This is why He did not protest (Compare with John 10:18; Matthew 11:29; 26:52-54; 27:12-14).

4) What fate should the Messiah have suffered after His death? What happened instead?

Isaiah 53:8-9

"By oppression and judgment he was taken away. Yet who of his generation protested? For he was cut off from the land of the living; for the transgression of my people he was punished. He was assigned a grave with the wicked, and with the rich in his death, though he had done no violence, nor was any deceit in his mouth."

These verses foretell the Messiah's unjust death. Crucifixion was a punishment that Romans reserved for slaves and foreign rebels. It was considered the most shameful punishment. A Roman citizen could not be crucified (thanks to this, the Apostle Paul, a Roman citizen (Acts 22:25-29), was beheaded, a quick and honorable death). On the cross, one could suffer for days before dying. Death came not so much from bleeding but from infections and suffocation. Killed as a godless person, He was destined to be dishonored even after His death, perhaps buried away from others, carelessly, just to get Him out of sight.

5) What hope allowed Jesus to accept all this? What is implicit in the examined text?

Isaiah 53:10-12

"Yet it was the LORD's will to crush him and cause him to suffer, and though the LORD makes his life an offering for sin, he will see his offspring and prolong his days, and the will of the LORD will prosper in his hand. After he has suffered, he will see the light of life and be satisfied; by his knowledge my righteous servant will justify many, and he will bear their iniquities. Therefore I will give him a portion among the great, and he will divide the spoils with the strong, because he poured out his life unto death, and was numbered with the transgressors. For he bore the sin of many, and made intercession for the transgressors."

Verse 10a:

The language used should not be understood literally according to today's meaning. It does not mean that God rejoiced in Jesus' sufferings. Rather, God "accepted" that the Messiah would suffer if this could bring salvation to His people. Just as Jesus, the Son of God, will be "satisfied" with the positive fruits of His sacrifice (v. 11), so the Father "rejoices" in the salvation that would come to us through His plan of salvation. The Father and the Son are united in their love for us.

Verses 10b, 11a:

If the Messiah died, all this can only happen by presupposing Jesus' resurrection. From His death comes our life, from His suffering our joy, from His apparent defeat the greatest success. Isaiah saw even the moment of victory beyond the pain of death.

Verses 11b, 12b:

The righteous Messiah takes on the sins of the people, but through this experience, His righteousness becomes the sinner's righteousness. It happens as an exchange totally favorable to us. The knowledge spoken of here is that of God's love which He shares and transmits to the sinner.

"To know God" means to be one with Him, to share His character, His will, His hope.

Verse 12:

Humiliation is replaced by glorification, defeat by victory, service by lordship. Paul probably thinks of Isaiah 53 when he describes the Lord's humiliation followed by His glorification (Philippians 2:5-11).

For Reflection:

  • Why did God choose to save us through Jesus' suffering and death?
  • What consequences does this have in our way of seeing God and living our lives?
