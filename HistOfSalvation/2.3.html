God Promises a Savior

Purpose:

To understand God's love who, though abandoned, does not abandon His creation. To comprehend how Jesus is the Savior promised since ancient times.

Introduction

After sin entered, God could have abandoned rebellious humanity to its fate of death, but He didn't because He loved them and continued to care for them. While He had to be severe as sin is severe, God opened the door of hope by promising a Savior. Here we will examine some Messianic prophecies in their chronological order.

1) When did God make the first promise?

Genesis 3:15

Immediately after sin, while the first couple was still in Eden, God promised them the coming of a Savior who would suffer death because of Satan but, through this very death, would defeat Satan. This was fulfilled in Jesus when He died on the cross, becoming the world's Savior.

2) What promise did God make to Abraham regarding his offspring?

Genesis 12:1-3

"In you all nations of the earth will be blessed." The Savior would come through Abraham's people, Israel.

3) From which tribe would the Savior come?

Genesis 49:10

"The scepter will not depart from Judah, nor the ruler's staff from between his feet, until he to whom it belongs shall come and the obedience of the nations shall be his." Judah would be the royal tribe until a mysterious person would come with the right to rule the entire world. This person is called Shiloh, or "The One who has the right." It's worth noting that Israel definitively lost its independence when Jesus came and became the spiritual king of the Jews and all humanity (John 18:33-38). He will come again a second time as the glorious King of kings and Lord of lords (Revelation 19:16).

4) Among the many families of Judah, from which would salvation come?

Isaiah 11:1

From the house of Jesse, father of King David. That the text speaks of the Savior is clear from the following verses describing God's new creation (vv. 2-9) through this person. Jesus was known as the "Son of David" (Matthew 1:1; 9:27; 21:9).

5) The Messiah would come to give His life for His people's sins

Isaiah 53:5

Isaiah 53 deserves deeper study and will be considered in detail in the next lesson.

6) Daniel's Prophecy of the Messiah's Coming

The Prophet Daniel tells us that the Messiah would come toward the end of a period of 70 symbolic weeks, or 490 literal years. This period begins with the royal decree to restore Jerusalem in 457 BC (Daniel 9:24,25). The final part of the prophecy begins in 27 AD, when Jesus was baptized and, as the prophet had foretold, manifested Himself as the expected Messiah and began His ministry (Luke 3:23). Jesus was then about 30 years old because there is an error in our calendar and Jesus was born around 4 BC. This prophecy also deserves more in-depth study and will be covered in another lesson (The Coming Messiah, Daniel 9).

Galatians 4:4

Additional Insights

Understanding Messianic Prophecies

All prophecies cited in this study definitely refer to a future Messianic reality and find their fulfillment in Christ. However, some texts typically considered Messianic might have acquired this meaning later. Two cases might have occurred:

1. Contemporary Application with Future Fulfillment

Some texts might simply express a reality concerning the writer or people of their time, but through their language, point to a higher reality. For example:

Isaiah 7:14

"Therefore the Lord himself will give you a sign: The young woman will conceive and give birth to a son, and will call him Immanuel."

  • Context indicates this child was a sign for the unbelieving people of the prophet's time
  • The Hebrew term 'almah' means "young woman" (who could be virgin before marriage)
  • The specific Hebrew term for virgin is 'bethulah'
  • Matthew 1:23 uses 'parthenos' (virgin) from the Greek Septuagint
  • The prophecy finds immediate fulfillment in Isaiah's time and ultimate fulfillment in Christ

2. New Testament Typological Reading

The New Testament views the entire Old Testament as testimony and preparation for the Messiah (John 5:39). For example:

Matthew 2:14-15 citing Hosea 11:1

"He got up, took the child and his mother during the night and left for Egypt, where he stayed until the death of Herod. And so was fulfilled what the Lord had said through the prophet: 'Out of Egypt I called my son.'"

  • Hosea's text originally refers to Israel's exodus
  • Matthew sees a parallel between Israel and Christ
  • Both sought refuge in Egypt
  • Both were delivered by God
  • Israel becomes a type/symbol of the Messiah
