6.3 Daniel 2: Unlocking the Future

Discovering the reality and meaning of biblical prophecies as evidence of a personal God who knows and controls history

Purpose

To begin discovering the reality and meaning of biblical prophecies as evidence of a personal God who knows and controls history, guiding it to a good end despite the existence of evil. The study of this specific prophecy will lay the foundation for understanding the rest of the book of Daniel and the plan of salvation.

Introduction

We finished the last study by asking whether it is possible to know the time of the final judgment symbolized by the Day of Atonement. The answer is yes, but we must follow a somewhat long path through the message of the book of Daniel. It won't be easy because it requires historical knowledge and careful attention. This is not a study for everyone, but God promises His blessing to those who listen to the prophetic word of this book and the subsequent book of Revelation (Daniel 12:4; Revelation 1:3).

1. Who was Daniel?

He was a young Hebrew taken to Babylon in 606-5 BC as a political prisoner. Because of his noble origin, he was welcomed into the court of Nebuchadnezzar, the king of Babylon, who wanted to make him a faithful servant, perhaps a qualified link with his people of origin. There, with God's help, the young man became part of the circle of Babylonian wise men, and God called him to transmit messages to the king himself, to subsequent kings, and to write words of encouragement for his people and for us.

2. How did it happen that the future was revealed to King Nebuchadnezzar?

Daniel 2:1-28

Read or summarize Daniel 2:1-28. Note how:

  1. The Babylonian magicians could not know the future (v. 10).
  2. Daniel did not seek to gain advantages over the magicians who had disappointed the king but sought to justify them (v. 27).
  3. Daniel was a humble person who only wanted to give glory to God (v. 28, 30).

3. What was the purpose of the dream given to the king?

Daniel 2:29-30

To help the king understand the final objective of the history of this world: the kingdom of God. Probably so that he would not overestimate the religious significance of his victories over the people of Israel, thinking it was also a victory over their God, and also to invite him to a humble view of his power and his personal function.

4. In what form is the history of the world revealed to the king and to Daniel?

Daniel 2:31-46

Note the following facts:

  1. Starting with Babylon, compared to the glory of gold, we have only 4 empires that directly succeed one another. History teaches us that these are the kingdoms of Babylon, Medo-Persia, Greece, and Rome.
  2. While the difference from one to another is described in terms of preciousness (from gold to iron), the last is certainly described in relation to its strength, its power: Rome perfectly matches this description; its empire was the most vast and strong among the described empires.
  3. The fourth empire is not followed by another that takes its place. Instead, it is divided into several kingdoms that will remain until the advent of a different and eternal kingdom, raised by God at the end of time. This is exactly what happened to the Roman Empire, reduced to fragments by barbarians who had gradually infiltrated its territories, being assimilated there and sometimes becoming the backbone of the armed forces.
  4. Several people have tried to reunify the Roman Empire but, until now, after about 1500 years since its dissolution, no one has succeeded. Some European countries are now trying to achieve political unity, but it is still only a portion of the ancient Roman Empire. One might also, perhaps, advance the hypothesis that, of these kingdoms, rather than emphasizing the territorial aspect of the former Roman Empire, one should emphasize the human aspect. The peoples who composed them are now scattered throughout the world, and the prophecy could simply mean that the world we know, and in which God's people live, will never be united as it was for the empires that anciently controlled ancient Israel and then the early Christian church.
  5. The only part of the prophecy not yet fulfilled is that relating to the stone that breaks off from the mountain without human hands (thus comes from God). It represents the kingdom of God that will be manifested at the return of Jesus. All other parts of the prophecy have been perfectly fulfilled, and after 2600 years we can say that no one has been able to disprove it. We only have to wait confidently for the fulfillment of the last part.

5. What can we learn through this simple prophecy?

  1. The Bible is truly the inspired word of God (v. 28) because no one could have revealed the future in such a clear and unequivocal way.
  2. If God knows everything before it happens, then we can also be at peace about our future because He knows our life and will be able to bring it to a good end, if we entrust it to Him.
  3. The world will not continue forever as it is now, and we need not fear that it will end with a natural catastrophe or a man-made upheaval. God will bring about a radical change and transform our reality into an immensely superior one. The kingdom of God is coming, and we can look to the future with confidence.

In-Depth Study

6. Why doesn't this biblical prophecy mention other important historical realities like ancient China or India?

The purpose of prophecy is not to predict the history of the world as such. For that, God would have had to give us an encyclopedia, not just a few lines. The purpose is instead to give the Babylonian king an idea of the future of his kingdom, thus outlining the empires that would subsequently follow it, until the kingdom of God is established. This history begins with Babylon because at that time it was the power that controlled Israel, God's people, and God is interested in His people. Nations like China or India had nothing to do with what we are seeing.

7. Some scholars describe the 4 kingdoms differently: Babylon, Media, Persia, Greece, the division of Greece, and the coming of the Messiah. Why do Adventists reject this interpretation?

Some reasons are as follows:

  1. When Media and Persia entered the prophetic scene, they were already unified and cannot be separated.
  2. The prophet Daniel himself, in another parallel prophecy, describes Media and Persia as a single power represented by a single animal (8:20).
  3. The above interpretation does not correspond to the ultimate purpose of the prophecy, which is to close history with the destruction of human kingdoms which are miraculously replaced by the kingdom of God. The falling stone cannot refer to the first coming of Jesus, which was peaceful, almost unnoticed, and the kingdoms of this world have continued to exist for 2000 years. Daniel describes the coming of God's kingdom in the same terms in which the Bible describes the return of Jesus: a rapid and worldwide event that everyone sees. When Jesus came the first time, He did not come to judge and destroy the nations but to announce salvation. The nations will disappear at the time of His return (Revelation 19:15-21).
  4. There would be no mention of the Roman Empire, which already existed at the time of Jesus' coming.
  5. This interpretation does not arise from historical data but from the fact that many modern scholars, influenced by modern humanistic rationalism, refuse to believe in the divine inspiration of the Bible and reduce it to merely the word of man. For them, Daniel was not a true prophet who lived in the court of Babylon before the announced events were realized, but a Jew who lived in the 2nd century BC who wrote, inventing the prophecy to encourage his people in a difficult time. In this way of understanding, the message of Daniel 2 must close at his time when the oppressive kingdom of the Seleucids, heir to the Greek-Macedonian empire, dominated. The coming of a savior then becomes only a pure human hope based on fantasy, something that as believers we cannot accept.
