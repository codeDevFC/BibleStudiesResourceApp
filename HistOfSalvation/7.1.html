7.1 - Following Jesus on Three Mountains

Understanding what it means to be a true Christian

Introduction +

What does it mean to be a Christian? There are many ways to answer this question. One answer could be: "Christians are those who follow Jesus." Many times Jesus invited people to follow Him (Matt 9:9; 28:19,20 – The main verb of this text is "make disciples." Disciples are not just people who believe in Jesus, but they follow Him, listen to Him, imitate Him).

To help us better understand and remember, we can say that Christians are those who follow Jesus on three mountains.

1. The Mount of Beatitudes (Matthew 5-7) +

a. Here we receive His blessing, His comfort, and His teaching.

b. Is it important to know Jesus' teaching? (John 20:30,31; Romans 10:16,17)

c. Mary and Martha: an example of the importance of listening to Jesus (Luke 10:38-42)

"Now it happened as they went that He entered a certain village; and a certain woman named Martha welcomed Him into her house. And she had a sister called Mary, who also sat at Jesus' feet and heard His word. But Martha was distracted with much serving, and she approached Him and said, 'Lord, do You not care that my sister has left me to serve alone? Therefore tell her to help me.' And Jesus answered and said to her, 'Martha, Martha, you are worried and troubled about many things. But one thing is needed, and Mary has chosen that good part, which will not be taken away from her.'" (Luke 10:38-42)

On this mountain, we learn to sit at Jesus' feet and absorb His teachings. Like Mary, we must make time to listen to the Master, even amidst our busy lives. His words provide the foundation for our faith journey.

2. The Mount of Transfiguration (Luke 9:28-36) +

a. The second stage of our journey with Jesus is that which leads us to see Jesus, not only as a good teacher, but as the incarnation of God's glory and power. Only by understanding this can we have hope that He can lead us to a better life and a better world.

b. Jesus' promise of eternal life in God's salvation. (John 3:16; Romans 8:18-25)

"Now after six days Jesus took Peter, James, and John his brother, led them up on a high mountain by themselves; and He was transfigured before them. His face shone like the sun, and His clothes became as white as the light. And behold, Moses and Elijah appeared to them, talking with Him... While he was still speaking, behold, a bright cloud overshadowed them; and suddenly a voice came out of the cloud, saying, 'This is My beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased. Hear Him!'" (Matthew 17:1-3, 5)

On the Mount of Transfiguration, we move beyond seeing Jesus merely as a teacher and recognize Him as divine. Peter, James, and John witnessed Jesus' glory, a glimpse of His true nature. Similarly, we must progress in our faith to understand Jesus' divinity and the transformative power He brings to our lives.

3. The Mount of Golgotha (Matthew 27:33) +

a. Jesus invites His disciples to follow Him also to Golgotha (Matt 16:24)

b. This is the mountain of sacrifice, of the total gift of self (Matt 10:38,39; 16:25)

"Then Jesus said to His disciples, 'If anyone desires to come after Me, let him deny himself, and take up his cross, and follow Me. For whoever desires to save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for My sake will find it.'" (Matthew 16:24-25)

Golgotha represents the ultimate challenge of Christian discipleship. Here, Jesus calls us not just to learn from Him or recognize His divinity, but to follow Him in the way of sacrifice. The cross symbolizes our willingness to deny ourselves, surrender our own desires, and commit fully to God's purposes, even when difficult or painful.

Questions for Discussion +
  1. Which mountain do you love the most? Why?
  2. Can we skip one of these mountains and continue to be Christians?
  3. In what order should we climb these mountains? Why?
  4. What might discourage us from completely following Christ on one of these mountains? How can we succeed? (John 10:27-29; Romans 8:35-39)

Think About It

Our journey with Jesus involves all three mountains. We need His teaching (Beatitudes), recognition of His divinity (Transfiguration), and willingness to follow in sacrifice (Golgotha). Which mountain presents the greatest challenge in your spiritual journey right now?

In-Depth Study

The Progressive Nature of Discipleship +

The three mountains represent a progression in discipleship. We typically begin by learning from Jesus' teachings, move to recognizing His divine nature and authority, and finally commit to the sacrificial life He models. However, these aren't strictly sequential—we may revisit each mountain throughout our Christian journey as we grow deeper in faith.

Jesus' approach to discipleship reflects this progression. He first taught the crowds (Sermon on the Mount), then revealed His glory to select disciples (Transfiguration), and ultimately demonstrated sacrificial love at Calvary. He invited followers to increasingly deeper levels of commitment based on their readiness.

This pattern appears throughout Scripture. Consider Peter's journey:

  • He first heard Jesus teach and witnessed His miracles (Matthew 4:18-20)
  • He witnessed the Transfiguration and declared Jesus as "the Christ, the Son of the living God" (Matthew 16:16)
  • After initially faltering at the crucifixion, he ultimately embraced sacrificial discipleship and tradition holds that he was crucified upside-down for his faith

The same progression can be seen in our own spiritual growth. We must be willing to follow Jesus to each mountain, allowing His work in our lives to transform us completely.

The Inseparable Nature of the Three Mountains +

While we can distinguish between these three mountains for learning purposes, in reality, they form an integrated whole in Christian discipleship. Jesus' teachings (Beatitudes) only make sense in light of who He is (Transfiguration) and what He did (Golgotha). Similarly, His sacrifice at Golgotha derives its meaning from His divine identity and teachings.

This explains why many who attempt to follow Jesus' ethical teachings while rejecting His divinity or sacrificial death ultimately find themselves unable to live out those teachings fully. The power to live the Christian life comes from all three mountains:

  • The Beatitudes provide the blueprint for Christian living
  • The Transfiguration reveals the divine power available to transform us
  • Golgotha demonstrates both the cost of discipleship and the depth of God's love that motivates our own sacrifice

As James writes, "Faith without works is dead" (James 2:26), and as Paul explains, we are "transformed by the renewing of your mind" (Romans 12:2). Complete Christian discipleship requires following Jesus to all three mountains.
