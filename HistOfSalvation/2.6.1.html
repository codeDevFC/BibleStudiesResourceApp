10. In-Depth Analysis of John 1:1-14

"The Word" (verse 1)

Jesus (v. 14) is called this because, as a word manifests one's inner thoughts, He manifests God's otherwise unknowable reality. Jesus can do this because He is "in the Father's bosom" and coming to us, reveals Him (v. 18).

"In the beginning"
  • References when God created the universe (Genesis 1:1)
  • Uses imperfect tense ("was") indicating ongoing existence, not a point of beginning
  • Demonstrates Jesus' pre-existence before creation

Important Theological Distinctions:

  • "The Word was with God" - Shows distinction from the Father
  • "The Word was God" - Shows divine nature
  • These statements together prevent both:
    • The error of denying Jesus' distinction from the Father
    • The error of making Jesus a mere creature

11. Understanding "Firstborn" in Colossians 1:15

Common Misunderstanding

Some interpret this to mean Jesus was the first created being.

Biblical Analysis

  1. Context shows Jesus as Creator, not creature
  2. Term indicates preeminence, not chronological order
  3. "Firstborn" denotes status and authority

Biblical Examples of "Firstborn" as Status:

  • David called "firstborn" though youngest son (Psalm 89:20,27)
  • Ephraim designated as firstborn though second son (Jeremiah 31:9)

13. Why Jehovah's Witnesses Created Their Own Bible Translation

The Jehovah's Witnesses are today's primary opponents of Jesus' deity. They deny His co-eternal nature with the Father. While honoring Him as a powerful and merciful being, they consider Him only as God's first creature through whom God then created other creatures.

Key Translation Comparisons:

John 1:1
Biblical Teaching:

"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."

JW Translation:

"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was a god."

Critical Analysis:
  • Original Greek manuscripts don't distinguish between upper and lowercase letters
  • Adding "a" before "god" introduces a polytheistic concept foreign to Scripture
  • Contradicts biblical monotheism (Romans 3:30; 1 Corinthians 8:6; 1 Timothy 2:5)
John 8:58
Biblical Teaching:

"Before Abraham was, I AM."

JW Translation:

"Before Abraham came into existence, I have been."

Critical Analysis:
  • Greek 'ego eimi' is present tense, deliberately echoing Exodus 3:14
  • Jesus claims the divine name "I AM" (YHWH)
  • The Jews' reaction (attempting to stone Him) confirms this understanding

Understanding Proverbs 8:22-23

"The LORD possessed me at the beginning of His way, before His works of old. I was established from everlasting, from the beginning, before there was ever an earth." (Proverbs 8:22-23)

Common Misinterpretation

Some use this text to argue that Jesus was created by God before the creation of the world. This interpretation is incorrect for several reasons:

  1. Context

    These verses and those following refer to wisdom personified (see verse 1), not to Jesus directly. Wisdom is represented as a woman teaching how to live wisely.

  2. Hebrew Term Analysis

    The Hebrew verb 'qanah' can mean:

    • To possess
    • To acquire
    • To create (in some contexts)

    In this context, "possess" is most appropriate as it would be paradoxical to suggest God created wisdom when He would need wisdom to create anything.

  3. Eternal Nature

    Verse 23 states wisdom was "established from everlasting" ('olam'), indicating eternal existence rather than a point of creation.

Understanding the Trinity Concept

Biblical Foundation

  • The term "Trinity" isn't found in Scripture
  • Developed to express biblical truth about God's nature
  • Represents church's understanding of divine revelation

Practical Understanding

While the term itself is extra-biblical, the concept it represents is thoroughly biblical:

  • One God existing in three persons
  • Each person fully divine
  • Distinct but not separate

Balanced Perspective

While acknowledging the term's legitimacy in Christian theology, we can:

  • Prefer biblical language when possible
  • Recognize its value in systematic theology
  • Focus on biblical truth rather than terminology
  • Maintain humility regarding divine mysteries
