Doctrine Of The church

4.3 The Lord's Supper

Purpose:

To understand the meaning of this Christian rite, second only to baptism, as a sign of grace and life received from Christ both individually and collectively.

Introduction

Every living being needs to eat and drink. We cannot live without an external source of energy. In spiritual life, it's the same: we need external power to become and be children of God. One of the most beautiful and effective ways Jesus used to teach us this lesson is what we call the Holy Supper, or the Lord's Supper.

Historical Background

It happened during the last evening of Jesus' earthly life. A few hours later, Judas would deliver Him to the soldiers to be judged and killed. It was a very solemn moment, and Jesus was celebrating Passover, a feast commemorating the miraculous deliverance through which God had led His people out of Egyptian slavery.

1. What Special Meaning Did Jesus Give to the Bread and Wine?

Matthew 26:26-28; Mark 14:22-25; Luke 22:15-20

During the Passover meal, unleavened bread and wine were used. Jesus gave them special meaning by comparing His body to the bread and His blood to the wine.

2. How Does Paul Describe Our Dependence on Jesus?

Galatians 2:20

"I have been crucified with Christ and I no longer live, but Christ lives in me." Do you also believe that Jesus is our life and we cannot live without Him?

3. Why Did Jesus Command to Celebrate the Lord's Supper?

Luke 22:19; 1 Corinthians 11:24,25

As a remembrance of His sacrifice. Why should we never forget what Jesus has done for us?

4. What Did Jesus Intend to Establish With This Rite?

Luke 22:20

The new covenant of grace in His death, through the sacrifice of His life given in love.

5. Why Does Jesus Call This a "New" Covenant?

Jeremiah 31:31-33

This new covenant was necessary because only through Christ's sacrifice can we understand that God is love, that His law is a sign of this love, and that it's worth living in faithful obedience to Him, loving Him fully!

Note: The New Testament explicitly connects the new covenant announced by Jeremiah with that established by Jesus (see Hebrews 8:8-12; 10:16).

6. What Are the Results of the New Covenant?

a. Better Understanding

We better understand God's love

John 3:16; 1 John 4:8-10

b. Obedience

We learn to obey God's commandments

John 14:15; Jeremiah 31:33

c. Forgiveness

Our sins are forgiven

Jeremiah 31:34; Matthew 26:28

d. Unity

We learn to love one another and be one body

1 Corinthians 10:17

7. What Importance Should This New Covenant Have for Us Personally?

This is an opportunity for personal reflection on the meaning and impact of the covenant in our lives.

8. What Other Rite Did Jesus Institute Before the Actual Supper?

John 13:1-15

The washing of feet, the rite of humility and purification.

Passover, of which the Lord's Supper is the ideal continuation, coincided with the Feast of Unleavened Bread (Exodus 12:1-21; Leviticus 23:5-8).

Important Considerations:

  • All leaven was to be avoided during this feast
  • The bread was unleavened because Passover symbolizes Christ, and nothing corrupted could represent His pure sacrifice (1 Corinthians 5:8)
  • Adventists believe this principle applies to both bread and wine
  • Unfermented grape juice is used as alcohol is discouraged in Scripture
John 13:1-15

1. Humility

In ancient times, washing someone's feet meant being their servant. By washing each other's feet, we declare ourselves servants to one another in love.

2. Confession

Accepting foot washing acknowledges our need for cleansing and forgiveness.

3. Baptism Renewal

When Peter asked to be fully washed, Jesus replied that one who is bathed needs only foot washing, symbolizing our ongoing need for grace after baptism.

  • Use unleavened bread and unfermented grape juice
  • Include foot washing service (men with men, women with women)
  • Typically celebrated quarterly
  • Each congregation free to establish convenient times

While some churches celebrate daily or weekly, and others annually during Passover, we believe no single biblical norm exists. Jerusalem Christians appeared to celebrate daily (Acts 2:46), but this was likely specific to their situation rather than a divine command.

Paul teaches that each person should examine their spiritual condition, not to abstain if problems exist, but to resolve issues and participate honestly.

Prerequisites:

  • Acceptance of Jesus as Savior
  • Baptism by immersion
  • Members of other Christian churches welcome if they share our understanding of the rite

No. They can only tell us which tradition they follow but not what God's will is. Our faith is founded on the Bible, and they have no special means to understand it better than we can.

Historical Insight:

No one can have mathematical certainty about what type of wine was used during Passover, but we know for certain that the Essenes were accustomed to drinking only sweet, unfermented wine. Since their purpose was to preserve the ancient priestly rites uncontaminated, their practice might have some value regarding our question.

They had received material bread (John 6:26; see vv. 1-14) and wanted more (John 6:30,31). They desired a Messiah who would satisfy their physical needs. Instead, Jesus teaches them that for eternal life, they needed to seek better food: the gift of His life.

Understanding the Teaching:

  • Jesus affirms His body is true food and His blood true drink
  • As stated in verse 63, these words must be understood spiritually
  • He is food for spiritual life, not for material benefits

Definition:

The word comes from Latin and means "to go beyond (trans) the substance." Catholic theologians use it to indicate their belief that when the priest pronounces the formula "this is my body... my blood," the true nature of the bread and wine changes, going beyond appearance, to become Jesus' flesh and blood. Only the external appearance of bread and wine would remain.

Historical Development:

  • Established as Catholic dogma in 1215 at the Fourth Lateran Council
  • Before this date, not all Christians believed in transubstantiation

Early Church Perspectives:

  • St. Augustine (c. 400): "Understand spiritually what I told you. You will not eat this body that you see, and you will not drink the blood that those who crucify me will shed."
  • St. John Chrysostom (344-407): "Before consecration we call it bread, but then... it loses the name of bread and becomes worthy to be called the Lord's Body, although the nature of bread continues in it."
  • Theodoret, Bishop of Cyrus (393-458): "The mystical symbols [bread and wine] do not abandon their nature after consecration but maintain their substance and form in everything as before."
  • Pope Gelasius I: "The sacrament of Christ's body and blood is truly a divine thing; but the bread and wine maintain their substance in the nature of bread and wine."
