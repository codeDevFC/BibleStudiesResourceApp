Doctrine Of The Church

Becoming a Member of the
Christian Church:
Baptism

Purpose:

To demonstrate that becoming a member of the Christian church isn't automatic through birth into a Christian society or family, but through conscious and free choice. Baptism is the sign of this choice and should be practiced after understanding and accepting its meaning.

Introduction

Becoming part of the Christian Church is an act of faith that requires instruction, awareness, love, and obedience. All Christians agree that baptism is the solemn act through which this reality is fulfilled and officialized, making us part of God's family. Unfortunately, not all Christians understand baptism in the same way, neither its form nor its meaning. For this reason, we must go to the Bible to learn what God has taught about this.

1. What Does "Baptism" Mean?

The word comes from the Greek "baptisma," meaning immersion. To be "baptized" literally means to be "immersed" in water, being completely covered by it. We'll soon see what this signifies.

2. Did Baptism Exist in the Old Testament?

No. There were many types of ablutions and washings, which could be partial (Exodus 40:31) or total (Leviticus 13:58; 15:19), but these didn't constitute the moment of entry into God's people. In Old Testament times, one became part of God's people through circumcision (Genesis 17:10-12; Exodus 12:48; Leviticus 12:3).

Those who were already circumcised practiced ablutions to purify themselves from some sort of hygienic or ritual contamination. However, the idea that through the water of ablutions one could obtain a condition of purity certainly prepared the way for the concept of Christian baptism as an instrument for obtaining purification from sins.

3. Who Was the First to Practice Baptism in the New Testament?

Matthew 3:1-11

John the Baptist, Christ's forerunner, was the one who introduced baptism in the New Testament era. People coming to John for baptism expressed humble awareness of being unclean, sinners, and asked God to purify and forgive them (v. 6). In this way, they were preparing for the Messianic age that Jesus would soon inaugurate.

Jews practiced baptism for Gentile converts to Judaism: being considered totally unclean, they needed to be totally washed. Thus, John, by asking Jews to be baptized, was asking them to renounce any moral superiority over pagans and recognize their need for salvation and grace just like them.

4. Did Jesus Practice Baptism?

Yes, Jesus himself went to John to be baptized (Matthew 3:13-15), even though He personally didn't need it, to identify Himself from the beginning of His ministry with sinful humanity (Hebrews 2:17) and perhaps to set an example. He then began baptizing people who came to Him, though He did this through His disciples (John 4:1-2).

5. Is Baptism a Christian Requirement?

Matthew 28:19-20

"Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you."

Jesus' final act before ascending to heaven was commanding His disciples to preach His teaching and baptize those who would accept it.

6. Who Can Be Baptized?

Biblical Requirements

Matthew 28:19-20

Jesus clarifies that being baptized equals becoming His disciple - someone who recognizes Him as teacher and follows Him. Verse 20 specifies they must also be taught "to observe all that I have commanded you."

Order of Events

Mark 16:15-16

The sequence is clear:

  1. Preach the gospel
  2. Believe
  3. Be baptized

Key Understanding

Only those who understand the meaning and value of Jesus, who know God and His will according to "all" that Jesus taught, and believe in Him... these are the ones who can and should be baptized. This perspective excludes baptism administered to infants who are unaware of what others are doing for and to them.

Biblical Example

Acts 8:37

"Philip said, 'If you believe with all your heart, you may.' And he replied, 'I believe that Jesus Christ is the Son of God.'"

This is why we define Christian baptism as a "believers' baptism."

7. In What Form Should Baptism Be Practiced?

Christians practice baptism in two fundamental forms: immersion and sprinkling. Here are the reasons why we consider only the first form valid:

1. Word Etymology

The word "baptism" itself means "immersion." A literal translation of the gospel would be: "Go and immerse people."

2. Biblical Evidence

Matthew 3:6

People went to the Jordan River for baptism by John.

Matthew 3:16

After being baptized, Jesus "came up" from the water.

John 3:23

John baptized at Aenon because there was "much water" there.

Acts 8:36-39

The Ethiopian eunuch waited for a place with enough water for baptism.

3. Symbolic Meaning

Romans 6:3,4

Paul presents baptism as a sign of burial with Christ, to be reborn to a new life. Only total immersion in water can symbolize a tomb in which one is buried.

4. Complete Cleansing

The fundamental meaning of baptism - being completely washed from our sins to be reborn to a new life - can only be coherently represented by total immersion in water.

Luke 3:3 1 Corinthians 6:11

8. What About Those Baptized as Infants by Sprinkling?

Many believers, educated in the Catholic Church or in some Protestant churches where infant baptism is practiced, face a serious dilemma when they discover the biblical meaning and form of baptism: should they consider themselves already baptized or not? Should they be rebaptized?

Biblical Perspective

While we cannot judge others' consciences, according to evangelical teaching, they have not yet been baptized, neither in:

  • Form (they were not "immersed")
  • Substance (they did not declare their faith through baptism as they were not aware of either their sin or the salvation and forgiveness offered by Jesus)

Others decided for them when they were children, but faith and obedience are something we must appropriate personally. No one can believe and obey in our place. Consequently, shouldn't they reclaim this privilege of personally obeying Jesus' command once they know the truth?

9. Is Baptism, in Itself, a Means of Salvation?

1 Peter 3:21

"Baptism, which corresponds to this, now saves you, not as a removal of dirt from the body but as an appeal to God for a good conscience."

No, in itself, baptism is only a ritual that should express:

  • Awareness of our sin
  • Trust in God's grace

Without these elements, it would become merely a way to remove "dirt from the body" - just a bath, we might say.

10. Can One Be Saved Without Baptism?

The Thief on the Cross

Received the promise of salvation although he could never be baptized. What saves is not baptism but faith in Jesus that it expresses (John 3:16).

Had he been able to come down from the cross and live a normal life, he would certainly have expressed his faith by accepting Jesus' command to be baptized like everyone else.

Unable vs. Unwilling

Unable to be Baptized

If we cannot be baptized due to circumstances beyond our control (imagine a very elderly and sick person), this does not prevent salvation.

Unwilling to be Baptized

If non-baptism stems from conscious refusal to obey Jesus, could we still claim to have faith in Him?

11. What Should We Do? An Example of Gospel Obedience

Acts 2:37-39

"When they heard this, they were cut to the heart and said to Peter and the other apostles, 'Brothers, what shall we do?'

Peter replied, 'Repent and be baptized, every one of you, in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins. And you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit. The promise is for you and your children and for all who are far off—for all whom the Lord our God will call.'"

Personal Application

If you have understood God's love for you and the meaning of baptism, are you ready to respond in the same way as these first hearers of the gospel?

If your answer is positive, then the promise of forgiveness and the gift of the Holy Spirit is also for you.

John's Baptism

Was a sign of repentance (Matthew 3:11) in anticipation of God's salvation, which was about to be manifested through Jesus.

Jesus' Baptism

Only in Jesus could God's love and saving power be fully manifested. This is why only Jesus could baptize with the Holy Spirit, establishing full communion between God and humans, because only through Jesus could the separation caused by sin (Isaiah 59:2) be eliminated and we could truly become "children of God" again (Romans 8:15,16; Galatians 4:6), receiving all His grace and gifts.

John 3:3-8

Jesus states that to "see" the kingdom of God, we must be born again, being baptized "of water and Spirit."

The Two Natures

The gospel speaks of two persons within us:

  • The Old: Accustomed to living far from God, following its own desires and ways. This person is condemned to death because "the wages of sin is death" (Romans 6:23).
  • The New: Has welcomed Jesus, rejoicing in His grace (Ephesians 2:4,5) and obeying His commandments (John 14:15). Its reward is eternal life (Romans 6:23).

The Dual Baptism

Two aspects of baptism address our need:

  1. Water Baptism: We must confess our corruption and ask God's forgiveness
  2. Spirit Baptism: We need God's Spirit to live in harmony with His will, illuminated by His wisdom (Ephesians 1:17; John 16:8) and enabled by His power (Romans 8:1,5,6)
Matthew 28:19

Jesus commanded to baptize "in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit."

Normal Process

When we are baptized in water, this act of faith is honored by the presence and baptism of the Spirit. However, only God knows the reality of our heart, if we have truly accepted Jesus, His teaching, and His grace.

Special Cases

  • Before Water Baptism: God may anticipate the Spirit's gift, as with Cornelius (Acts 10:44-48)
  • After Water Baptism: Sometimes occurs later, as with the Samaritans and some of John's disciples (Acts 8:14-17; 19:1-6)

Gospel Evidence

Acts 2:37-39

Peter announced that if they repented and were baptized in Jesus' name, they would receive the Holy Spirit's gift.

If we have truly repented of our sins and genuinely accepted Jesus Christ in our life, we can be certain of the Spirit's baptism, because where Jesus is, there is also the Spirit.

Personal Experience

Galatians 4:6

We know we are God's children, born of the Spirit, because we can confidently approach God and call Him "Father."

Visible Fruits

Galatians 5:22-26

If the Spirit's fruit is in us, manifesting signs of genuine Christian conversion in our life, this indicates God's Spirit is in us.

Common Misconception

Some believers think Spirit baptism always manifests through speaking in tongues - the miraculous ability to instantly speak unknown languages, whether human or angelic.

Biblical Perspective

  1. In Acts, "tongues" were known human languages that listeners could understand (Acts 2:8,11)
  2. Paul clearly states not all Christians receive the same gift (1 Corinthians 12:10,11)
  3. The emphasis on tongues in Acts relates to:
    • Enabling gospel preaching to all nations
    • Proving Gentiles received the same Spirit as Jews

The true model for Christian experience is found in John 20:19-23, where Jesus appeared to His disciples post-resurrection, giving them:

  • The Spirit
  • Assurance of His victory over death
  • The mission to announce forgiveness of sins

Yes and no: it depends on what we mean.

Matthew 28:19,20

Jesus asked His disciples to make people His disciples, baptizing them and teaching them all He had commanded.

Basic Understanding

This doesn't mean we need perfect knowledge of all biblical revelation details - no one could ever be baptized if that were the case. However, we should understand the fundamental elements of Christian faith.

Today's Context

  • Miracles are rare or potentially deceptive (2 Thessalonians 2:9)
  • Teaching comes from humble human instruments, not apostles
  • Christianity is divided with varying interpretations
  • Careful reflection needed to distinguish truth from tradition

For honesty and clarity, we choose not to baptize anyone without a clear and complete presentation of our faith. This requires time, but the effort is rewarded with richer knowledge and stronger faith.

Personal Reflection

Key Questions to Consider:

  • Have you understood the biblical meaning of baptism?
  • Are you ready to make this commitment to follow Christ?
  • Do you understand the importance of both water and Spirit baptism?
  • Are you prepared to join God's family through baptism?

If you feel ready to take this important step or have questions about baptism, speak with your Bible instructor or pastor.
