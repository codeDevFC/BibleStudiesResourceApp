DOctrine Of The Church

4.5 Peter & The Foundation of The Christian Church

Purpose:

To free Christian faith from the idea that the church can be founded on a human being and to reaffirm Christ's exclusive position as the foundation of the church.

Introduction

We have so much in common with Catholic believers, but there are also many differences that we cannot honestly ignore. One of the most important concerns authority within the church. The Catholic Church teaches that Jesus gave the church a visible head in Peter and his successors (the bishops of Rome) as the "rock" upon which all Christianity should stand. Instead, we believe that the foundation stone of the church is not a man but Jesus Himself, and that we find reliable guidance in God's Word. Let's see what the Bible says.

1. Did Jesus Found a Church?

Matthew 16:18

Certainly! "I will build my church."

2. When Jesus Says to Peter, "You are Peter (petros), and on this rock (petra) I will build my church," Who is the "Rock" on Which the Church Will Be Built?

The text is complex, and here we offer a traditional explanation. To understand what Jesus meant, we must consider both immediate and general context:

A. The Temple Analogy

Jesus is comparing the Christian Church to a solid temple built on rock.

B. Living Stones

The apostle Paul, using a similar image, says this temple is made of living stones, people who accept Jesus as their savior (Ephesians 2:18-22). He also says Jesus is the cornerstone from which this temple is built. The apostle Peter offers the same teaching (1 Peter 2:2-8).

C. Becoming a Stone

Following this image, used by both Christ and the apostles, a person becomes a stone in the church when accepting Jesus as Savior who comes from God. Peter had just confessed this (Matthew 16:16), and it's this testimony of faith that allows Jesus to define Peter as a "petros," a stone in His church.

D. Word Choice Significance

This doesn't mean Peter is the Rock on which the church is founded. If Jesus had wanted to say this, He would have said: "You are petros and on this petros I will build my church." Instead, He says: "You are petros (meaning 'stone, rock') and on this petra (meaning 'solid rock') I will build my church."

E. Conclusion

The rock on which the Christian church stands is not a man but Christ Himself, whom we must recognize and confess, following Peter's example, as Messiah, the Savior, Son of the living God.

3. Is This Interpretation Something Modern Invented to Contradict Papal Authority?

No, it's based on God's Word and existed in the Catholic Church itself for more than a thousand years.

Historical Record

Archbishop Peter Richard Kenrick, in a pamphlet prepared for the Vatican Council, lists 5 interpretations given by the early Church Fathers:

  • 17 fathers say the rock is Peter
  • 8 say it is formed by all the apostles
  • 44 say it is Peter's faith, not his person
  • 16 say it is Jesus Himself whom Peter had confessed
  • Others say it is formed by all believers who believe Christ is God's Son

From "An Inside View of the Vatican Council, 1872, pp. 107-109"

Even a Pope's Understanding

Even Pope Gregory VII (1073-85) knew that the Rock was Jesus and not Peter, as seen in a famous inscription sent with a crown to Rudolf of Swabia: "Petra dedit Petro, Petrus diadema Rudolpho." Meaning: "The Rock (Jesus) gave it to Peter, and Peter (in the person of the Pope) now gives the crown to Rudolf."

From Philip Schaff, History of the Christian Church, 1902, Vol. 3, p. 303

4. In Matthew 16:19, Jesus Gives Peter the Keys of the Kingdom of Heaven - Is This Authority Given Only to Peter?

Matthew 16:19

"I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven; whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven."

A. The Keys' Meaning

Jesus Himself teaches that what opens heaven's door is knowledge of the gospel (Luke 11:52). Thus, the keys given to Peter are the authority and privilege to preach Jesus' gospel.

John 6:68 1 Peter 1:23 Romans 10:13-17

B. Shared Mission

The mission to preach the gospel was entrusted to Peter not because he was special but because he had accepted Jesus. By accepting Jesus, we become both church members and His witnesses.

Compare Ephesians 2:20-22 with 2 Corinthians 3:3; 1 Peter 2:5 with verse 9

C. Church Discipline

Sometimes, based on gospel knowledge, the church is called to judge a person's fitness for heaven's kingdom. In this case, binding or loosing equates to what we call "church discipline." Such discipline isn't entrusted to a single person but to the entire Christian community.

Matthew 18:15-18

D. Ultimate Authority

While the church can be an instrument to open kingdom doors, only Jesus truly holds the keys (Revelation 3:7), and the church can only humbly seek to interpret His will.

5. In John 20:19-23, the Risen Christ Gave the Disciples the Privilege to Forgive or Retain Sins. Doesn't This Give Bishops and Priests Special Authority?

A. Collective Authority

We don't believe so. As with the Matthew text already considered, we note here that Jesus doesn't attribute any privilege to one or a few people, but to all who believe in Him. In this John text, Jesus isn't addressing only Peter or the apostles, but all disciples gathered in the room, including the two who had met Jesus on the road to Emmaus (Luke 24:33).

B. Gospel Connection

Once again, as with previous texts, what Jesus says about forgiving sins is correlated to gospel preaching (John 18:21). Thus, Jesus gave all disciples not arbitrary power to decide who should or shouldn't be forgiven, but the privilege and responsibility to share with others the knowledge of the gospel that alone can save from sin's condemnation.

6. Can We Find Proof of This Fact?

Yes! Neither Peter nor any other apostle ever claimed to forgive anyone by their own authority. Instead, they invited people to repent and go to God for forgiveness.

Acts 8:20-22 Acts 2:38 Acts 5:31 Acts 10:43 Acts 13:38 Acts 26:15-18

7. Should We Then Say That Peter Has No Authority in the Christian Church?

No, we don't say this. But his authority is different from what Catholics attribute to him. Consider these facts:

Apostolic Witness

Peter was chosen to represent Jesus by giving testimony of His life and teaching (Luke 6:13; Acts 1:21,22): he has the apostolic authority of being an authorized witness of Jesus. However, he shares this authority with other apostles.

Shared Foundation

This teaching is confirmed by Paul when he says the Church is built on the foundation of apostles and prophets, "with Christ Jesus himself as the chief cornerstone" (Ephesians 2:20).

Equal Standing

While Peter is called a pillar of the church, he shares this title with James and John (Galatians 2:9), and Revelation 3:12 says this of all who overcome their spiritual warfare for Christ.

8. If the Pope and Bishops Are the Apostles' Successors, Doesn't This Mean We Must Submit to Them?

Apostolic Requirements:

What made apostles special was their particular relationship with Jesus. As we know from their own history and the procedure adopted in Acts 1:21-26 to seek Judas's replacement, they had to:

  1. Be eyewitnesses of Jesus' ministry
  2. Be chosen directly by Him

Neither the pope nor bishops present these characteristics and cannot be considered apostolic successors. The Bible doesn't provide for any papal function, and as for bishops, also called "elders" (Acts 20:17, 28; 1 Timothy 4:14), theirs is a ministry different from the apostolic one.

9. How Then Can We Build Ourselves on the Foundation of Apostles and Prophets?

The apostles' main purpose was to testify to Jesus' teaching. As such, their ministry doesn't end with their death. Their teaching was written down and is still available to us today. The Bible is the word of prophets (Old Testament) and apostles (New Testament). By listening to the Bible, we honor the ministry the Lord entrusted to these people and become part of Jesus' true church.

The difference between petros and petra exists only in the Greek text used by Matthew to tell the story, while Jesus' original language was Aramaic, in which there is no difference between the two words.

Our Response:

If Matthew, an apostle of Jesus perfectly capable of understanding any Aramaic used by the Lord, chose to distinguish the two words, this means he:

  • Recognized the possibility of misunderstanding the Master's words
  • Guided by the Holy Spirit, avoided such possibility
  • Gave us the exact meaning of Jesus' teaching

Matthew is the only available reality, and on its basis alone can and must we develop our understanding of Christ's teaching and the Christian church itself.

The entire discourse on Peter's primacy, on which the concept of papal authority rests, presupposes that Peter was the first pope and that as such he governed over the whole church in his function as bishop of Rome. However, this view conflicts with biblical and historical data.

A. Early Church Leadership

Each Christian community was initially guided by a college of elders or bishops

Acts 15:6 Acts 20:17,28 1 Timothy 4:14 Titus 1:5 James 5:14

Only later, from the second century, did a leader emerge among these elders, with others becoming assistants.

B. Historical Development

  • After the apostles' death, for several centuries, each community was independent
  • Gradually, bishops of larger churches extended influence over smaller surrounding communities
  • Over time, Rome's church and its bishop emerged, eventually becoming the Papacy

Interpreting Peter's figure and role in light of these later historical developments is entirely improper.
