Doctrine of Man

Our Hope in Face of Death

Purpose:

To discover the biblical hope of resurrection as the reverse process of death and as victory over it.

Introduction

Death is a sad reality. God did not create us for it but for life: this is why we cannot love it: it is an enemy (1Cor 15:26).

Common Belief

Many think that at death only the material body dies while the spiritual soul is immortal and continues to live in a different condition - in paradise, hell, or purgatory.

Alternative View

Others believe in reincarnation. But God teaches us something different.

1) Is man, or his soul, immortal?

The Bible denies that human beings or their souls are immortal:

  • Romans 2:7 - Man seeks immortality (therefore does not yet possess it)
  • 1 Timothy 6:16 - God alone possesses immortality
  • Matthew 10:28 - The soul (psychè) can be "killed" in the fire of Gehenna

2) What is death without Jesus?

References: Genesis 2:7; 3:19; Ecclesiastes 12:7

Note that the biblical text doesn't say that only the body returns to dust but "you", meaning our entire person.

  • Ecclesiastes 9:5,10 - Unconsciousness and inactivity: everything that constitutes human life disappears. Death is the absence of life.
  • Psalm 115:17 - The dead cannot praise God.

3) What does death become for those who believe in Jesus?

John 11:11-15

With Jesus, death is no longer the end of life but a sleep, a rest, from which we will wake at the dawn of resurrection.

  • 1 Thessalonians 4:13-18 - The dead in Christ sleep awaiting His return
  • Daniel 12:2,3,13 - The hope of resurrection in the Old Testament

4) What nature will we have at the resurrection?

1 Corinthians 15:42-44

We will have a spiritual body, one that is in harmony with the laws of the Spirit, not an evanescent body.

  • We will eat and sit on thrones (Luke 22:30)
  • We will recognize each other
  • We will likely be similar to the risen Christ (Philippians 3:20-21; 1 John 3:2; John 20:26,27; Luke 24:41-43)

5) How can we imagine the resurrection?

Ezekiel 37:1-11

The resurrection is an extraordinary miracle: the Creator will recreate our glorious and vital body.

6) How does the Apostle Paul describe the resurrection of the righteous?

1 Corinthians 15:49-54

"And as we have borne the image of the earthly, we shall also bear the image of the heavenly... Behold, I tell you a mystery: We shall not all sleep, but we shall all be changed— in a moment, in the twinkling of an eye, at the last trumpet. For the trumpet will sound, and the dead will be raised incorruptible, and we shall be changed. For this corruptible must put on incorruption, and this mortal must put on immortality... then shall be brought to pass the saying that is written: 'Death is swallowed up in victory.'"

7) When will the righteous be resurrected?

1 Thessalonians 4:16,17

"For the Lord Himself will descend from heaven with a shout, with the voice of an archangel, and with the trumpet of God. And the dead in Christ will rise first. Then we who are alive and remain shall be caught up together with them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air. And thus we shall always be with the Lord."

See also 1 Corinthians 15:52

8) Let's schematize the entire process of life we have studied:

Stage Description Biblical Reference
Creation Dust + Breath of Life = Living Being Genesis 2:7
Death Living Being - Breath of Life = Dust Ecclesiastes 12:7
Resurrection Dust + Breath of Life = New Living Being 1 Corinthians 15:42-44

9) Who was the first to deny the reality of death?

Genesis 3:4

The first lie ever told relates to the nature of man and death. Satan told it, "the father of lies" (John 8:44). He tried to convince humanity that death didn't exist, and we must admit he has been very successful.

The truth we have just learned protects us from many deceptions and sufferings. At the same time, we have the certainty that in Jesus death is conquered and soon our mortal being will receive immortality.

  • How important does Jesus' return become in this perspective?

11) How can we understand King Saul's experience consulting the spirit of dead Samuel?

1 Samuel 28:6-20

The Bible often narrates stories as they appear without expressing judgment. The judgment comes from the reader's comparison with God's teaching.

  • Saul's experience went against God's will, which prohibited contact with those claiming to communicate with the dead (Deuteronomy 18:11)
  • Ancient law prescribed death for such practices (Leviticus 20:6,27)
  • Saul himself had previously enforced this order (1 Samuel 28:9)

Important Observations:

  • Saul never saw anyone; he likely didn't hear anyone directly
  • The medium acted as an intermediary, describing what she claimed to see and hear
  • Satan, knowing past events well, could impersonate Samuel
  • This encounter led Saul to final despair and suicide (1 Samuel 28:19; 31:4)

12) What about the "good thief" crucified with Jesus?

Luke 23:43

Key Evidence:

  • John 20:17 shows that three days after His death, Jesus had not yet ascended to the Father
  • Since God is in heaven (Matthew 6:9), paradise and heaven are synonymous in New Testament language
  • Jesus couldn't have promised the thief paradise that Friday

Translation Note:

Ancient manuscripts had no punctuation. The text literally reads: "Truly to you I say today you will be with me in paradise"

Placing the comma after "today" resolves the apparent contradiction:

"Truly I say to you today, 'You will be with me in paradise.'"

This interpretation aligns with the thief's request to be remembered when Jesus comes into His kingdom (Luke 23:42), referring to Christ's future return in glory (Matthew 24:30; Luke 21:27).

13) What did Paul mean by his desire to leave his body and be with God?

2 Corinthians 5:1-8

Paul uses symbolic language that shouldn't be taken literally. Note how he carefully avoids speaking of a soul leaving the body.

Understanding the Metaphors:

  • Tent = temporary earthly existence
  • Building = eternal existence God prepares
  • Clothed = being alive
  • Naked = being dead

This passage follows 2 Corinthians 4:13-18, where Paul clearly expresses his hope in resurrection, not soul immortality.

14) What about Jesus preaching to the dead?

1 Peter 3:18-20

This doesn't mean Jesus preached to the dead while they were dead. Instead:

  • Jesus preached to these people when they were alive, during Noah's time
  • He did this "in the spirit" through Noah, who was a "preacher of righteousness" (2 Peter 2:5)
  • Alternatively, Christ may have preached to Noah's generation before His incarnation

The purpose of this reference is to affirm that salvation through Jesus isn't limited to one particular time: all have had opportunity for salvation, both before and after Christ's death.

15) Why does Peter compare his death to leaving his tent?

2 Peter 1:13,14

"Leaving my tent" simply means leaving this transitory condition of life, dying.

Biblical Parallel:

Isaiah 38:12: "My dwelling is pulled up and removed from me like a shepherd's tent. Like a weaver I have rolled up my life; He cuts me off from the loom."

Neither Peter nor Paul say that destroying this mortal tent means going to heaven. The metaphor simply means that one dies.

16) How do we understand the souls under the altar in Revelation?

Revelation 6:9-11

Understanding the Symbolism:

  • Revelation uses predominantly symbolic language
  • These martyrs' souls are not conscious entities
  • The reference is to their blood, following Old Testament imagery

Important Connections:

  1. These souls are at the altar's base, referencing where sacrificial blood was poured in the sanctuary (Leviticus 4:7)
  2. In the Old Testament, blood is life, and life is "nephesh" (soul) (Genesis 9:4)
  3. This blood "speaks" figuratively, like Abel's blood crying to God (Genesis 4:10)

These "souls" are told to "rest" a little longer - connecting to the sleep of death mentioned by Jesus (John 11:11-14) and Paul (1 Thessalonians 4:13)

17) How do we interpret Paul's desire to depart and be with Christ?

Philippians 1:20-26

Understanding Human Expression:

We must be careful with human language, which is often ambiguous and imprecise. Even today, though not believing in soul immortality, we might say someone "has left us" when they die.

Key Observations:

  • Paul never mentions a soul leaving the body
  • He contrasts present life's struggles with future life in Christ
  • His hope focuses on personal communion with Jesus in eternity

Compare with 1 Thessalonians 5:10-11 where Paul refers to the resurrection hope, "whether we are awake or asleep" (alive or dead)

18) Our Hope in Face of Death

Key Biblical Truths:

  • Death is an enemy, not a friend (1 Corinthians 15:26)
  • In Christ, death becomes temporary - like sleep
  • Our hope lies in resurrection, not soul immortality
  • This resurrection occurs at Christ's return

Practical Impact:

  • Greater appreciation for the gift of life
  • Deeper understanding of God's salvation plan
  • Freedom from false teachings about death
  • Increased anticipation of Christ's return

This biblical understanding provides both comfort and truth, grounding our hope in God's actual promises rather than human traditions.
