5.4 The Ten Commandments

Understanding God's moral law and its application in Christian life

Introduction +

God's law is a complex reality. When the Bible uses this term, it can refer to different parts of revelation. There are moral laws as well as ceremonial, agricultural, hygienic, and political laws. Sometimes "Law" refers to a particular norm, a group of norms, the five books of Moses, or the entire Old Testament revelation. Each of these laws has its value, but not all have the same spiritual significance and scope. Each must be understood in its specificity.

The Unique Nature of the Ten Commandments +

Divine Origin

Exodus 31:18

Written directly by God, while others were written by Moses (Exodus 34:27)

Sacred Storage

Exodus 25:16; Hebrews 9:4

Kept inside the Ark of the Covenant, while other laws were placed beside it (Deuteronomy 31:26)

Eternal Value

1 Corinthians 7:19

They have eternal value, while others (like ceremonial laws) may have temporary significance

Understanding the Ten Commandments +

Preface to the Commandments

God is a God of freedom. His is a law of liberty (James 1:25; 2:12). We don't observe it because we're forced by external pressure, but because Jesus has loved us so much, establishing with us a new covenant of grace and faithfulness (Compare Luke 22:20 with Jeremiah 31:31-33). Through this covenant of love, God's law is now written in our hearts, and we observe it out of love (2 Corinthians 5:14).

I. No Other Gods

Having only one God means worshiping and following only the true God. Anything, even good things, can become false gods when we give them more importance than God:

  • Matthew 6:24 - Money
  • Matthew 10:35,37 - Parents and children
  • Philippians 3:19 - Food

II. No Graven Images

The commandment isn't against images themselves, but their use in God's service, to prevent:

  • Material representation of God
  • Substitution of God with objects
John 4:24

Jesus teaches to worship God in spirit and truth

Example: The bronze serpent (Numbers 21:9; 2 Kings 18:4) - Even divinely commanded objects can become idols when misused.

III. God's Name in Vain

We must respect God. He is our Father and we love Him, but if we treat Him as one of us, what extraordinary thing can He do for us?

Matthew 6:9

As Jesus teaches in the Lord's Prayer, God is our father and loves us, but He remains in heaven and above us.

IV. Remember the Sabbath

The Sabbath rest is intended as a blessing for us, to reclaim our freedom and dignity as God's children and brothers of others in a world that is God's gift to love and respect.

This topic will be studied in detail in subsequent lessons.

V. Honor Parents

Matthew 15:4-6

Honor includes practical support when needed

2 Timothy 3:2

Disobedience to parents indicates moral corruption

Ephesians 6:1-4

Parents should avoid dictatorial and oppressive attitudes

VI. Do Not Kill

Matthew 5:21,22, 38-48

We are called to love and forgive. Christian non-violence prevents us from accepting capital punishment and using weapons against our enemy.

The Seventh-day Adventist Church is a non-combatant church.

VII. Do Not Commit Adultery

Adultery breaks an eternal covenant of love and solidarity. One who loves cannot be adulterous.

Genesis 1:26; 2:21-25 Matthew 5:27,28 Malachi 2:14; 3:5

VIII. Do Not Steal

We must respect others' property, but there are many ways to steal.

James 5:1-4

Examples of indirect stealing through unjust practices

IX. Do Not Bear False Witness

John 14:6

We are called to live in harmony with Jesus, who is the truth

John 8:44

Liars align with Satan, the father of lies

X. Do Not Covet

1 Timothy 6:6-10

Having what is sufficient for life, we should be content

Think About It

The Ten Commandments aren't just rules to follow, but principles that reveal God's character and His desire for human flourishing. How might viewing them as expressions of God's love change your approach to obedience?
