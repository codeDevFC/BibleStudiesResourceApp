7.2 - Marriage

Understanding God's design for the sacred covenant of marriage

Introduction +

Marriage is probably the most important human and Christian institution. The very life of the human race depends on it, as well as much of the joy or, unfortunately, the sorrows we experience individually. Even from a spiritual point of view, a good marriage constitutes an important foundation for our success. Unfortunately, in our society, marriage risks losing its original meaning and importance. As Christians, we must consider it very carefully, praying to God to help us understand and live this experience in harmony with His will.

1. Marriage in Eden (Genesis 1:26-28) +
  1. Marriage is the first institution created by God and part of a perfect reality.
  2. Both man and woman are created in God's image.
  3. Both male and female are "man," human beings.
  4. Marriage is the framework for procreation (see Gen 2:24).
  5. In summary: God creates the human couple giving both equal dignity and calling both to be His collaborators in managing creation.

"Then God said, 'Let Us make man in Our image, according to Our likeness; let them have dominion over the fish of the sea, over the birds of the air, and over the cattle, over all the earth and over every creeping thing that creeps on the earth.' So God created man in His own image; in the image of God He created him; male and female He created them. Then God blessed them, and God said to them, 'Be fruitful and multiply; fill the earth and subdue it; have dominion over the fish of the sea, over the birds of the air, and over every living thing that moves on the earth.'" (Genesis 1:26-28)

Genesis 2:18-25 teaches, with a different but compatible image, that:

  1. Between man and woman there is a unique bond incomparable with all others.
  2. The woman represents an aspect ("rib" in Hebrew also means "side, aspect") of the human being: she is bone of his bones and flesh of his flesh. As such, the woman must be loved and honored, not exploited.
  3. Sexual life is the expression of the unity of the couple, not just a means of procreation.
  4. Marriage constitutes the creation of a new social nucleus through separation from parents (not abandoning them).

Fundamental elements of Christian marriage:

  1. Relationship with God, creator of marriage (religious aspect).
  2. Relationship between the two partners (affective and sexual aspect).
  3. Relationship with society (social aspect).
2. Marriage after the Fall +

Genesis 3:12,16 (consequences of sin)

  1. Division instead of unity, strength and power instead of love.
  2. The subordination of the woman to the husband should not necessarily be understood as an expression of God's will, but probably as a logical consequence of the state of corruption of human relationships that we saw in the previous point. However, we can also suppose that, in a state of abnormality, there was a need for order and God established one starting from the actual situation.

Deuteronomy 24:1 (Divorce)

  1. The overbearing nature of man makes woman something to exploit and, sometimes, abandon.
  2. God's law does not legitimize this situation but tries to control and limit it, giving the woman at least the right to a legal document that sanctioned her freedom from the old husband. The drafting of such a document also implies recourse to authorities and puts the man in a position of having to reconsider before implementing his purpose.

Exodus 34:15-16 (mixed marriages)

  1. God is against marriages with unbelievers because this undermines our faith and is a source of unhappiness (see also Ezra 9).
  2. Following the ancient biblical model, the Adventist Church, while respecting the conscience of its members, discourages marriage with members of a different faith, even if they are Christians. Marriage should represent a bond of total unity and communion: how could a couple be united without sharing the same faith, the most important element in the life of a sincere believer? This can happen when the differences between one faith and another are only formal, but the substantial differences between an Adventist and another believer are such and so many as to create inevitable difficulties in enjoying a totally happy unity.
3. Marriage in the Prophetic Books +
  1. Marriage is used as an image of the relationship between God and His people (Ezekiel 16:4-15; Jeremiah 2:2).
  2. They emphasize the necessity of forgiveness (Hosea 2:13-15).
  3. They are against any form of mutual exploitation (Malachi 2:14).

"'Return, backsliding Israel,' says the LORD; 'I will not cause My anger to fall on you. For I am merciful,' says the LORD; 'I will not remain angry forever. Only acknowledge your iniquity, that you have transgressed against the LORD your God, and have scattered your charms to alien deities under every green tree, and you have not obeyed My voice,' says the LORD." (Jeremiah 3:12-13)

The prophets consistently use marriage as a metaphor for God's covenant relationship with Israel. Just as marriage requires faithfulness, forgiveness, and mutual respect, so does our relationship with God. When Israel was unfaithful, God—like a wounded spouse—called for repentance and offered reconciliation, demonstrating the sacred nature of covenant relationships.

4. False Aspects of Marriage +
  1. Adultery (Exodus 20:14)
  2. Pre-marital sex (1 Corinthians 7:2)
  3. Cohabitation
  4. Sexual perversions (Romans 1:22-27)

Why are these realities contrary to God's plan for our marriage?

  1. Adultery breaks the solidarity of the couple.
  2. Pre-marital relationships imply a physical unity devoid of the responsible and socially affirmed and recognized solidarity that marriage implies.
  3. Cohabitation is not even foreseen as a negative possibility in the Bible. It represents a form of fear, of doubt, in the face of the marriage bond which instead presupposes a total love for the other (until death separates).
  4. Perversions destroy the nature created by God.

Think About It

God's design for marriage isn't meant to restrict our freedom but to protect the sacred bond that reflects His relationship with His people. How might viewing marriage as a covenant rather than a contract change how we approach relationships?

5. Jesus and Marriage +
  1. He honors marriage by performing His first miracle at a wedding (John 2:1-11).
  2. He invites people to return to the Edenic value of marriage, before the hardness of man's heart corrupted the relationship between human beings, and explains what is the only possible cause of divorce: adultery, that is, the fact that it is the other who interrupts the bond of solidarity (Matthew 19:1-9).
  3. To counter the tendency toward easy divorce, He presents the anomaly of an abandoned woman who remarries (Matthew 5:30-32). We suggest, on a personal note, that this saying of Jesus can be understood in light of Numbers 30:10-15, where a woman can be considered in a state of sin for not having honored a vow because of her husband's opposition, but she does not bear the responsibility which falls instead on the husband himself.

"And I say to you, whoever divorces his wife, except for sexual immorality, and marries another, commits adultery; and whoever marries her who is divorced commits adultery." (Matthew 19:9)

Jesus elevated marriage to its original divine intent, challenging the cultural norms that had degraded it. While recognizing the reality of human brokenness, He emphasized that God's ideal remains lifelong, faithful union. His teachings on divorce were not meant to trap people in abusive or broken relationships, but to protect the vulnerable (often women in that culture) and to highlight the sacred nature of the marriage covenant.

6. Legitimate Causes for Divorce +
  1. Matthew 19:9 (fornication or adultery)
  2. 1 Corinthians 7:15 (abandonment by the partner)

"But if the unbeliever departs, let him depart; a brother or a sister is not under bondage in such cases. But God has called us to peace." (1 Corinthians 7:15)

While Scripture upholds the permanence of marriage, it also recognizes that in a fallen world, relationships can be irreparably broken. The Bible identifies two specific circumstances where divorce may be permitted: sexual infidelity and abandonment by an unbelieving spouse. These exceptions acknowledge that when one party fundamentally violates the marriage covenant through unfaithfulness or desertion, the innocent party is not obligated to remain bound to that broken covenant.

However, even in these cases, reconciliation remains the preferred outcome whenever possible. Divorce is never commanded, only permitted in these specific circumstances, and the decision should be approached with prayer, counsel, and deep consideration.

7. Paul and Marriage in Ephesians 5:22-33 +
  1. In Christ, the husband must be honored as head of the family.
  2. In Christ, the wife must be honored and loved as Christ loved the church.

"Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ also loved the church and gave Himself for her, that He might sanctify and cleanse her with the washing of water by the word, that He might present her to Himself a glorious church, not having spot or wrinkle or any such thing, but that she should be holy and without blemish. So husbands ought to love their own wives as their own bodies; he who loves his wife loves himself." (Ephesians 5:25-28)

In summary: in Christian marriage, the social relationships that derive from our culture are respected, but they are filled with love and mutual service.

Paul's teaching on marriage provides a revolutionary framework that transforms the cultural norms of his day. Rather than simply reinforcing the patriarchal structures of Roman society, Paul calls husbands to a sacrificial love modeled after Christ's self-giving for the church. This love involves nurturing, protecting, and prioritizing the well-being of one's spouse.

The wife's submission is not about inferiority or blind obedience, but a voluntary yielding within a relationship of mutual submission (Ephesians 5:21). The husband's headship is not about domination but about servant leadership. Together, these complementary roles create a partnership that reflects Christ's relationship with the church—characterized by love, respect, and shared purpose.

8. Grace +
  1. Marriage is, in itself, grace, a gift from God that must be lived with gratitude and joy.
  2. Grace, in a world of sin, becomes forgiveness in case of errors: "Jesus, standing up and seeing no one but the woman, said to her, 'Woman, where are those accusers of yours? Has no one condemned you?' She said, 'No one, Lord.' And Jesus said to her, 'Neither do I condemn you; go and sin no more.'" (John 8:10-11). Without such a willingness to forgive, no marriage can last.

Think About It

Marriage requires grace—both the grace we receive from God and the grace we extend to our spouse. Just as Christ forgives us despite our failures, we must practice forgiveness within marriage. How might a spirit of grace transform conflicts in marriage?

The concept of grace is central to Christian marriage. It reminds us that none of us is perfect, and we all fall short of God's ideal. Just as God extends grace to us through Christ, spouses must extend grace to one another through forgiveness, patience, and unconditional love.

This grace doesn't excuse harmful behavior or remove accountability, but it provides the foundation for healing, growth, and renewed commitment. A marriage infused with grace becomes a powerful testimony to God's redemptive work in human relationships.

In-Depth Study

Marriage as a Covenant Relationship +

Throughout Scripture, marriage is presented not merely as a contract but as a covenant. Unlike contracts, which are based on mutual benefit and can be broken when one party fails to fulfill their obligations, covenants are characterized by unconditional commitment, faithfulness, and sacrificial love.

In Malachi 2:14, God refers to marriage as a "covenant," using the same Hebrew word (berith) used to describe His relationship with Israel. This covenant perspective transforms how we understand marriage:

  • Permanence: Covenants are intended to be permanent, not temporary arrangements.
  • Sacrificial Love: Covenant relationships are maintained through sacrificial love, not just when it's convenient or beneficial.
  • Witness: Marriage covenants are made before God and witnesses, emphasizing their sacred and community nature.
  • Grace: Covenants include provisions for forgiveness and restoration when failures occur.

Understanding marriage as a covenant helps us resist the consumer mentality that has infected modern relationships, where partners are valued only as long as they meet our needs. Instead, covenant marriage calls us to reflect God's faithful, enduring love even through difficulties.

The Spiritual Significance of Marriage +

Paul reveals in Ephesians 5:32 that marriage contains a "profound mystery" that reflects Christ's relationship with the church. This theological understanding elevates marriage beyond a mere human institution to a living parable of divine love.

This spiritual dimension of marriage has several implications:

  • Purpose: Marriage serves not only personal fulfillment but also spiritual witness, revealing God's character and plan to the world.
  • Unity: The "one flesh" union of marriage (Genesis 2:24) represents not just physical unity but spiritual oneness, with spouses growing together in faith and purpose.
  • Sanctification: Marriage becomes a context for spiritual growth as spouses help each other become more Christ-like (Ephesians 5:26-27).
  • Eschatological Hope: Christian marriage points forward to the ultimate "wedding" of Christ and His church (Revelation 19:7-9), giving it eternal significance.

This spiritual understanding doesn't diminish the human aspects of marriage—companionship, intimacy, family formation—but rather infuses them with deeper meaning. When spouses view their relationship through this lens, everyday interactions become opportunities to experience and demonstrate God's love.
