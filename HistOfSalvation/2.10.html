Our Eternal Home in the New Earth

Purpose:

To show the earthly nature of God's kingdom after the millennium as a restored earthly reality. To understand the beauty of God's plan for His children and prepare to be part of it.

Introduction

What kind of life will we have in God's kingdom? Will it be purely spiritual experience, filled only with contemplation, or will it also be a practical, active life?

1) What kind of life had God prepared for humanity before sin?

Genesis 1:26-28; 2:8

God prepared a wonderful world for humans where everything was perfect (Genesis 1:31). In this world, humans could live in harmony with nature and themselves. They acted as God's representatives and cared for it. Their life was active and joyful.

2) Is there any reason to think reality will be different on the new earth?

Romans 8:18-25

The Bible never presupposes an ethereal reality. We will continue to live on a new "earth," restored to its original beauty, and we will live in this restored world.

3) How does the prophet Isaiah describe the reality of the saved and their world?

Isaiah 11:1-7

A secure life in a restored environment.

Important Note:

"The knowledge of the LORD will fill the earth" means nothing will exist that isn't in total harmony with God and thus with the rest of creation. As we've seen elsewhere, "knowledge" in the Bible refers to an experience of deep unity with what is known (Genesis 4:1,17; Jeremiah 22:15,16).

Three Key Passages from Isaiah:

Isaiah 65:17-25

Life will again be active and joyful in a secure environment. Work isn't a problem but a way to express our creativity as God's collaborators. Sin transformed work into an often painful and alienating experience (Genesis 3:17-19), but in the renewed earth God is preparing, there will be no cause for pain.

Consider: Children work hard through their play, yet they're happy and grow through what they do. Imagine what we can learn and become with eternity before us!

Isaiah 35:1-10

No sickness, no reason for fear, no suffering.

Note: The absence of lions or wild beasts doesn't mean they won't exist, but that they won't be fierce anymore. (See Isaiah 11:6; 65:25)

4) What is the New Testament's vision?

Matthew 8:11

We will have a physical life that includes eating and drinking. (See also Matthew 26:29)

Revelation 21:1-5

We already know that at Jesus' return, all the saved will be taken to heaven where they'll spend 1,000 years. We now discover that after this long period, God will descend from heaven to our planet and establish His throne among humans. In a sense, earth will become "heaven," the center of the universe. Our planet has known the tragedy of sin but, by God's grace, it was also the place where God showed the greatest extent of His love. One day, by that same grace, earth will have the privilege of being the place where God manifests the glory of His presence. Isn't this truly a great reward for all our pains?

5) What's new in the New Testament's perspective?

In both Testaments, the home of the saved is a concrete, physical reality, with humans caring for the world where everything is in harmony with God and the rest. The New Testament simply specifies better and elevates the Old Testament promises to an even higher level.

The Only Real Difference:

  • Old Testament: Envisions primarily agricultural life
  • New Testament: Describes life in an immense, glorious city (the New Jerusalem)

This difference likely reflects the different social contexts in which Isaiah and John lived, with God conveying hope in ways each could relate to their heart's needs.

What We Can Learn:

We shouldn't imagine eternity only according to fixed, limited schemes. What matters is that however we live, we'll live in communion with God, loving one another, full of joy and satisfaction. We can imagine the new earth as God's opportunity for us to realize our most beautiful desires, things we could never achieve due to our current life's limitations.

Think about it: What would you like to do in the new earth?

6) Can we fully understand what this reality will be like?

1 Corinthians 2:9

Important Note:

This text doesn't directly refer to future realities but to the extraordinary and incredible greatness of what God has already done for us through Christ (see context). However, we can certainly apply this text to everything God does for us, because:

  • His thoughts are always higher than ours (Isaiah 55:8,9)
  • His power and creativity surpass anything we can imagine

7) What must we do to inherit all this?

Revelation 21:7

Only overcomers will inherit these things:

  • Overcomers of sin
  • Overcomers of difficulties
  • Overcomers of persecutions

We can be among these overcomers, by God's grace. We can be if we accept Jesus wholeheartedly and become His disciples (Romans 8:36-39).

Additional Insights

8. Will There Still Be Sin and Death on Earth According to Isaiah?

Isaiah 65:20 might give the impression these could still exist, albeit marginally, in the new creation. However, this doesn't seem to be Isaiah's intention because in other texts he clearly states:

  • No harm will be done in the new earth (Isaiah 11:1-9)
  • There will be no death (Isaiah 25:8)

Possible Explanations:

Transitional Phase Theory

While the other texts speak of the fully established new earth, Isaiah 65:20 might refer to a hypothetical transition phase that could have occurred if Israel had accepted the Messiah and His teaching.

Language Limitation Theory

A more complex but perhaps truer explanation appeals to human language limitations: When trying to understand someone, we should focus not so much on details but on the intended purpose. From this perspective, Isaiah simply wants to convey the sense of a serene life, described using the best possibilities we know today.

  • A life where even dying would be beautiful if one dies full of days (Genesis 25:8; 35:29)
  • Nothing truly impedes enjoying the joy God gives His children
  • This doesn't mean people will actually die

9. How Can We Understand the Description of the New Jerusalem in Revelation 21-22:5?

Most likely (this is the personal conviction of these notes' author) we can understand it as a parable of God's people and their life.

21:1 - The Sea

  • May represent the fearful reality it represented in the ancient world
  • No sea means no more danger, perhaps no divisions among God's children
  • Might reference the flood's aftermath (Genesis 7)
  • "No more sea" could mean overcoming current earth conditions affected by the flood's ecological disaster

21:2 - The Beautiful Bride

The most tender, affectionate, and glorious image representing God's people glorified by grace and their life conditions. The church is Jesus' bride-to-be (2 Corinthians 11:2).

21:3,22 - Sanctuary but No Temple

Understanding the Distinction:
Sanctuary (Present)
  • Dismountable tent symbolizing God's presence
  • Represents God's desire to share His love
  • Greek root means "to dwell" (Exodus 25:8)
  • Connected to Jesus' incarnation (John 1:14)
Temple (Absent)
  • Represents separation between holy and unholy
  • Not needed where everything is holy
  • Suggests separation requiring priestly mediation
  • Unnecessary in perfect communion with God

21:12-17 - Measurements and Numbers

The Number 12:
  • 12 foundations
  • 12 gates
  • 12 angels
  • 12,000 stadia per side
  • 144 (12x12) cubits wall height

Represents the fullness of God's saved people (like the 144,000 of Revelation 7:4; 14:1,3)

22:1-2 - The Tree of Life

  • Central in Eden (Genesis 2:9)
  • Lost through sin (Genesis 3:22,24)
  • Restored in New Jerusalem
  • Draws power from river of life flowing from God's throne
  • Represents Christ: "I am the life" (John 14:6)

10. Will We Remember Past Things in the New Earth?

Some Believe:

The memory of evil and suffering cannot coexist with perfect peace, citing Isaiah 65:17: "The former things will not be remembered, nor will they come to mind."

Biblical Understanding:

  • Memory loss would mean identity loss
  • Would eliminate awareness of salvation through Jesus
  • Would remove meaning from Christ's cross and love
  • Would eliminate basis for eternal gratitude

The text means our memories will be healed, consoled as God wipes away our tears (Revelation 7:17; 21:4). Like a mother who remembers childbirth pain but rejoices in new life, we'll remember but without oppression. Our awareness of history remains, but won't burden us. The vision of more beautiful things will occupy our thoughts.
