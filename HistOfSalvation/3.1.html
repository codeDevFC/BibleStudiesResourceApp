Doctrine of Man

What is Man?

Purpose:

To discover the beauty and coherence of the biblical message about human nature. To free the biblical view of humanity from elements derived from paganism. Given the consequences this doctrine has regarding the view of death and hope of eternal life (and many Catholic doctrines, such as those of saints, Mary, purgatory, hell, etc.), we understand the difficulties some may encounter and identify with their struggles. It's important to note that this doctrine doesn't exclude the hope of eternal life but grounds it in the hope of resurrection, as we'll see in the next study.

Introduction

"When I consider your heavens, the work of your fingers, the moon and the stars, which you have set in place, what is mankind that you are mindful of them, human beings that you care for them?" (Psalm 8:3-4)

Yes, before the universe, humans are small and weak. Yet, at the same time, we are great enough to contemplate the entire universe and its Creator. We are the only creatures on Earth capable of this. "What is man?", "Who am I?" How many times have we asked ourselves these questions!

Materialistic View

Some say we are the accidental result of chemical elements meeting, and these same elements will randomly dissolve at our death.

Spiritual View

Many others say what matters is not our material body but the spiritual soul. Most religions (Buddhists, Hindus, animists, and many Jews, Muslims, and Christians) believe this.

Who is right? Is there another possibility? Who can know better than the God who created us? Let us turn with confidence to His Word.

2) What comes from dust?

Evidently the physical reality of our being, our body.

3) What does "breath of life" mean?

Hebrews tended to think in concrete terms, not abstract ones like we do. This is why they speak of "breath of life" instead of just "life". Another concrete way of indicating life is blood (Genesis 9:4): as long as we breathe and blood circulates in us, we live.

Linguistic Understanding:

In our translation, the Hebrew neshamah (nishmat) (or the Greek equivalent pneuma) are sometimes translated as "breath," "spirit." It refers to everything unseen but possessing vitality, that moves or causes movement, lives or gives life.

Illustration:

Just as electricity is an impersonal reality producing different effects based on the devices it passes through, so the breath of life is the same for all living creatures but produces different results in each, based on their nature.

4) What is the living being (or living soul)?

It is the totality of the human being: body, thoughts, love, desires... Usually nefesh refers to a living being without any need to specify it. Sometimes it also refers to a dead person (Leviticus 19:28). In this case, a dead soul is opposed to a living soul.

Biblical Understanding:

In the biblical perspective, humans are not a composite reality but a unity or, as we say today, a holistic reality. Consider some uses of the word "soul-nephesh" (Genesis 1:20; Numbers 5:2).

Think About It:

Should we say we "have a soul" or that we "are a soul"?

5) What is the difference between humans and animals?

Humans are created in God's image (Genesis 1:26,27).

6) Does the biblical perspective reduce human value?

"Yet you have made them a little lower than God and crowned them with glory and honor" (Psalm 8:5)

Human value isn't calculated based on our component elements but on what God was able to make of us.

Illustration:

Think about the different appearance and value of a diamond versus a pencil lead. Both are made of the exact same substance, carbon. The only difference lies in how their atoms are positioned: randomly in the lead, precisely ordered in the diamond.

7) What's the best way to calculate human value?

Jesus, God's Son, offered His life to save even a single person (1 Peter 1:18).

8) What consequences can we draw from the biblical view of humans?

  • Greater coherence with modern scientific view of humans (psychosomatic)
  • Better understanding of human-nature relationship (ecology: we're part of the same world)
  • Revaluation of human body regarding dignity, health, sexuality, etc.

9) Where does the usual dualistic conception of humans come from?

Not from the Bible but from pagan philosophy. The Greek philosopher Plato was one of its best expositors. He thought the soul was like a bird trapped in the body's cage. Death was the moment when the cage dissolved and the bird could fly free.

10) A Problem: If in the biblical perspective we don't have an immortal soul, what happens at death?

We will consider this important question in our next study.

Additional Insights

11) Can we know more about the biblical meaning of "soul"?

Old Testament

Hebrew word: "nefesh"

New Testament

Greek word: "psychè"

While Greeks and Hebrews had different understandings of human beings, the New Testament, though written in Greek, was written by Hebrews who believed in Old Testament biblical teaching. Therefore, when we find "psyché" in the New Testament, we should understand it according to Hebrew thought, not Greek philosophy.

12) Does "soul" only mean the totality of the human?

Usually this is its primary meaning. "My soul" simply means "myself" or "I." However, there are other meanings in both the Old and New Testaments.

Key Points to Remember:

  • None of these uses refers to something that exists outside the body
  • The meaning must be determined by context
  • The New Testament, though written in Greek, maintains Hebrew thought patterns

13) Understanding 1 Thessalonians 5:23

The Biblical Text:

"May your whole spirit (pneuma), soul (psychè) and body (soma) be kept blameless at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ."

Key Principle:

Spirit, soul, and body are not three separate parts of our being. Rather, they represent three aspects of our single human nature ("your whole being").

The Three Aspects Explained:

Spirit (pneuma)

The capacity to conceive religious meaning in life

Referenced in:

  • 1 Corinthians 2:13
  • 1 Corinthians 3:1
  • Galatians 6:1

Soul (psychè)

The capacity to feel and understand as a human being

Referenced in:

  • Jude 1:19

Body (soma)

Expression of our ability to relate to nature through:

  • Physical touch
  • Eating
  • Walking
  • Other physical interactions with creation

Important Application:

When Scripture speaks of being "carnal" (1 Corinthians 3:1), it refers to living as purely animal beings, without spiritual values. This demonstrates how these aspects interrelate in human nature.

Theological Perspectives

From Roland De Pury (Reformed Theologian)

Alle origini della libertà. Tentazione di Gesù e condizione umana, Torino, Claudiana 1967 -pp.101,102,118.

"In opposition to all religions, the psalmists and sages, the witnesses of the Old Testament are unanimous: death leads to nothing and certainly not to God. It is not a place of communion, but of separation; not return, but abandonment; not an exit, but a pit."

"Death offered no compensation in Israel... it was not, as in other nations, a consoling reality, a transit to a better world, a liberation of the soul escaping from the body like a caged bird..."

"[Jesus' disciples] do not believe in the immortality of the soul. Man is not half mortal and half immortal; he forms a whole. Death is total; salvation too can only be total."
