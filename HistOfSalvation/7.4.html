7.4 - Angels

Understanding the biblical truth about heavenly messengers

Introduction +

Are we alone in the universe? Not at all! Not because we believe in so-called UFOs (whose philosophy and messages they offer are a clear satanic counterfeit, when they are not the result of simple fantasy) but because the Bible tells us that God has created other creatures that we call angels. What can we know about them?

1. Did God create them before or after humans? +

"Where were you when I laid the foundations of the earth? Tell Me, if you have understanding. Who determined its measurements? Surely you know! Or who stretched the line upon it? To what were its foundations fastened? Or who laid its cornerstone, when the morning stars sang together, and all the sons of God shouted for joy?" (Job 38:4-7)

In this passage, angels are called "sons of God." While God is questioning Job about where he was during creation, the text mentions that "the morning stars sang together, and all the sons of God shouted for joy." This indicates that angels were present to witness the creation of the earth, suggesting they were created before humans.

Angels are part of God's created order, not eternal beings like God Himself. They were created before the physical universe as we know it, and they witnessed with joy as God formed the earth and its inhabitants.

2. What kind of nature do they have? +

"Jesus answered and said to them, 'The sons of this age marry and are given in marriage. But those who are counted worthy to attain that age, and the resurrection from the dead, neither marry nor are given in marriage; nor can they die anymore, for they are equal to the angels and are sons of God, being sons of the resurrection.'" (Luke 20:34-36)

At the resurrection, we will be like the angels having a spiritual body. (See 1 Corinthians 15:42,44)

Angels are spiritual beings who do not marry or reproduce as humans do. They are immortal—they "cannot die anymore." While they can take physical form when necessary to interact with humans, their natural state is spiritual rather than physical.

This passage also gives us insight into our future resurrected state, when we will be "equal to the angels" in certain respects, particularly in having immortal, spiritual bodies not subject to death or the need for reproduction.

3. Besides being spiritual beings, what other characteristics do they possess? +

"Bless the LORD, you His angels, who excel in strength, who do His word, heeding the voice of His word." (Psalm 103:20)

Angels are characterized by:

  • Strength: They "excel in strength," possessing power far beyond human capability.
  • Obedience: They "do His word," faithfully carrying out God's commands.
  • Attentiveness: They are "heeding the voice of His word," always attentive to God's instructions.

Angels are not weak, passive beings but mighty servants of God who carry out His will with power and precision. Their strength is not used for their own purposes but is always directed by God's commands. They model perfect obedience, instantly responding to God's directives.

4. Even though they are so powerful, how do they behave toward us? +

"But to which of the angels has He ever said: 'Sit at My right hand, till I make Your enemies Your footstool'? Are they not all ministering spirits sent forth to minister for those who will inherit salvation?" (Hebrews 1:13-14)

Despite their power and exalted position, angels serve as "ministering spirits" sent to help believers—"those who will inherit salvation." Rather than lording their power over humans, they use it to serve and protect God's people.

This reveals God's care for His children. He assigns powerful heavenly beings to assist us in our journey of faith. Angels do not seek worship or recognition but faithfully fulfill their role as servants, both to God and to His people.

Think About It

Consider that at this very moment, powerful heavenly beings may be at work in your life, protecting and guiding you according to God's will. How might this awareness change your perspective on the challenges you face?

5. How did Jacob perceive their service? In what way can they help us too? +

"Then Jacob departed from Beersheba and went toward Haran. He came to a certain place and spent the night there, because the sun had set; and he took one of the stones of the place and put it under his head, and lay down in that place. He had a dream, and behold, a ladder was set on the earth with its top reaching to heaven; and behold, the angels of God were ascending and descending on it. And behold, the LORD stood above it and said, 'I am the LORD, the God of your father Abraham and the God of Isaac; the land on which you lie, I will give it to you and to your descendants... Behold, I am with you and will keep you wherever you go, and will bring you back to this land; for I will not leave you until I have done what I have promised you.' Then Jacob awoke from his sleep and said, 'Surely the LORD is in this place, and I did not know it.'" (Genesis 28:10-16)

Jacob perceived angels as messengers traveling between heaven and earth, carrying out God's purposes. In his dream, he saw angels "ascending and descending" on a ladder between heaven and earth, revealing that God's messengers are actively engaged in human affairs.

This vision gave Jacob assurance of God's presence and protection during his journey. Similarly, angels can help us by:

  • Providing protection in times of danger
  • Delivering guidance or messages from God
  • Offering encouragement and strength in difficult circumstances
  • Serving as a reminder of God's constant care and involvement in our lives

Jacob's response—"Surely the LORD is in this place, and I did not know it"—reminds us that God's presence and angelic activity often occur without our awareness.

6. Angels were able to comfort Jacob because there is a ladder that unites heaven and earth. Who does this ladder represent? +

"And He said to him, 'Most assuredly, I say to you, hereafter you shall see heaven open, and the angels of God ascending and descending upon the Son of Man.'" (John 1:51)

Jesus identifies Himself as the fulfillment of Jacob's ladder. He is the connection between heaven and earth, the mediator between God and humanity. Just as the ladder in Jacob's dream provided a way for angels to move between heaven and earth, Jesus provides the way for communication and relationship between God and humans.

This reveals an important truth: angelic ministry operates through and because of Christ. The work of angels in our lives is part of Christ's mediatorial role. They serve at His command and operate within His authority as the one who bridges the gap between heaven and earth.

This also helps us understand that we don't need to seek angels directly—we have direct access to God through Christ, who coordinates all heavenly ministry on our behalf.

7. Who is the chief of angels? +

"And it came to pass, when Joshua was by Jericho, that he lifted his eyes and looked, and behold, a Man stood opposite him with His sword drawn in His hand. And Joshua went to Him and said to Him, 'Are You for us or for our adversaries?' So He said, 'No, but as Commander of the army of the LORD I have now come.' And Joshua fell on his face to the earth and worshiped, and said to Him, 'What does my Lord say to His servant?' Then the Commander of the LORD's army said to Joshua, 'Take your sandal off your foot, for the place where you stand is holy.' And Joshua did so." (Joshua 5:13-15)

Compare this text with Exodus 3:2-6 (This is a very special "angel." He is called "The Angel of God" and asks to be worshiped as God. He is Jesus, the Son of God and God Himself. He is always called the angel and not an angel of God because He is unique. He is called "angel" because this word means "messenger" and Jesus is God's Messenger par excellence, His very word (John 1:1).

The Commander of the LORD's army who appeared to Joshua accepted worship and declared the ground holy—responses that would be inappropriate for a created angel but fitting for deity. This, along with similar Old Testament appearances, points to Jesus Christ in a pre-incarnate form.

As the eternal Son of God, Jesus has always been the head of the angelic hosts, commanding their activities and receiving their worship. This helps us understand the hierarchy of heaven—all angels, regardless of rank, ultimately answer to Christ.

8. Can we worship angels? +

"Then he said to me, 'Write: "Blessed are those who are called to the marriage supper of the Lamb!"' And he said to me, 'These are the true sayings of God.' And I fell at his feet to worship him. But he said to me, 'See that you do not do that! I am your fellow servant, and of your brethren who have the testimony of Jesus. Worship God! For the testimony of Jesus is the spirit of prophecy.'" (Revelation 19:9-10)

"Now I, John, saw and heard these things. And when I heard and saw, I fell down to worship before the feet of the angel who showed me these things. Then he said to me, 'See that you do not do that. For I am your fellow servant, and of your brethren the prophets, and of those who keep the words of this book. Worship God.'" (Revelation 22:8-9)

These passages clearly show that angels refuse worship. When John fell down to worship the angel who had shown him these revelations, the angel immediately corrected him, directing him to "Worship God" instead.

Angels understand their role as servants of God, not objects of worship. They recognize that worship belongs to God alone. This stands in stark contrast to Satan, who seeks worship that rightfully belongs only to God (Matthew 4:9).

This teaching protects us from the error of angel worship, which has appeared in various forms throughout history. True angels will always direct our attention and devotion to God rather than to themselves.

9. Can we pray to them? +

"Be anxious for nothing, but in everything by prayer and supplication, with thanksgiving, let your requests be made known to God." (Philippians 4:6)

They are our friends but, to avoid transforming them into gods, which they do not desire at all, we direct our prayers only to God, their and our Lord. In the Bible, angels are never the object of prayer. It is good that we ourselves follow this model. If God wills, it will be God who sends His angels to help us as He did with Daniel (Daniel 9:21-23).

Scripture consistently directs our prayers to God alone. Jesus taught us to pray "Our Father in heaven" (Matthew 6:9), not to angels or saints. While angels serve us, they do not receive or answer prayers—this is God's prerogative alone.

This distinction preserves the proper order of creation and prevents confusion about the role of angels. They are messengers and servants, not mediators between us and God. Christ alone fills that mediatorial role (1 Timothy 2:5).

Think About It

Rather than praying to angels, we can thank God for their ministry in our lives and ask Him to send angelic help according to His will. This maintains the proper relationship between humans, angels, and God.

10. What will angels do when Jesus returns? +

"Then the sign of the Son of Man will appear in heaven, and then all the tribes of the earth will mourn, and they will see the Son of Man coming on the clouds of heaven with power and great glory. And He will send His angels with a great sound of a trumpet, and they will gather together His elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other." (Matthew 24:30-31)

At Christ's return, angels will play a crucial role in gathering believers from across the world. They will accompany Christ in His glorious return, serving as His attendants and messengers.

This final angelic mission completes their role in the plan of salvation. Throughout history, they have announced God's messages, protected His people, and fought spiritual battles. At the end, they will help bring God's people home to Him.

THINK ABOUT IT: Can we imagine the moment when the angels will take us to heaven to meet Jesus?

This future reality gives us hope and anticipation. The same angels who have ministered to believers throughout history will one day escort us into the presence of Christ. This will be the culmination of their service to us—bringing us safely to our eternal home.

In-Depth Study

Special Stories About Angels +

2 Kings 6:8-23 - Angels protect Elisha and his servant from the Syrian army.

When Elisha's servant was terrified by the surrounding enemy forces, Elisha prayed, "LORD, I pray, open his eyes that he may see." The Lord opened the young man's eyes to see the mountain full of horses and chariots of fire surrounding Elisha—angelic forces protecting them.

Daniel 10:4-14 - An angel helps Daniel understand God's plan for Israel.

An angel, delayed by spiritual warfare with "the prince of the kingdom of Persia," finally reached Daniel to explain visions concerning Israel's future. This passage reveals angels' involvement in spiritual battles that affect earthly kingdoms.

Luke 1:26 - Gabriel is sent to Mary to announce the birth of Jesus.

The angel Gabriel delivered the most important announcement in history—that Mary would bear the Messiah. His words, "Rejoice, highly favored one, the Lord is with you," began the earthly chapter of our salvation story.

Luke 2:8-14 - Angels announce to the shepherds the birth of Jesus.

A multitude of the heavenly host praised God at Christ's birth, revealing heaven's joy at the Incarnation and demonstrating angels' role as heralds of God's greatest acts.

Acts 5:17-20 - An angel frees the apostles from prison.

When the apostles were imprisoned for preaching, "an angel of the Lord opened the prison doors" and instructed them to continue their ministry, showing angels' role in advancing the gospel.

Acts 12:7 - An angel frees Peter from prison.

While the church prayed earnestly for Peter, an angel appeared in his cell, struck him on the side, removed his chains, and led him out of prison—demonstrating God's sovereign power working through angelic ministry.

These accounts show the variety of ways angels serve in God's plan—protecting, guiding, announcing, and delivering. They reveal God's active involvement in human affairs through His heavenly messengers.

Is There an Angel Taking Care of Us? +

Hebrews 1:14 allows us to think this: "Are they not all ministering spirits sent forth to minister for those who will inherit salvation?"

The early Christians thought so: "Then they said to her, 'You are beside yourself!' Yet she kept insisting that it was so. So they said, 'It is his angel.'" (Acts 12:15)

When Peter was miraculously released from prison and knocked at the door where believers were praying for him, they initially thought it was "his angel"—suggesting a belief in personal guardian angels.

While the Bible doesn't explicitly teach that each person has an assigned guardian angel, several passages suggest that angels do watch over believers:

  • Psalm 91:11-12: "For He shall give His angels charge over you, to keep you in all your ways. In their hands they shall bear you up, lest you dash your foot against a stone."
  • Matthew 18:10: "Take heed that you do not despise one of these little ones, for I say to you that in heaven their angels always see the face of My Father who is in heaven."

Whether God assigns specific angels to individuals or simply sends angels as needed, the biblical teaching is clear that God uses angels to protect and guide His people. The focus should remain on God's care for us rather than on identifying or communicating with specific guardian angels.

Other Biblical Texts +

Galatians 3:19 - "Law promulgated through angels." (Acts 7:53)

The law was "appointed through angels by the hand of a mediator." Angels played a role in the giving of the Law at Mount Sinai, highlighting the solemnity and divine authority of God's commandments.

1 Peter 1:12 - Angels long to understand the plan of salvation.

"Things which angels desire to look into." Angels, though present at creation and involved in God's work throughout history, do not fully comprehend the mystery of salvation. They observe with wonder as God's redemptive plan unfolds through Christ and the church.

These additional insights reveal that angels are not omniscient—they continue to learn about God's ways. They serve as witnesses to God's work in human history, particularly His plan of redemption through Christ. Their interest in our salvation reminds us of the profound significance of what God has done for us in Christ.
